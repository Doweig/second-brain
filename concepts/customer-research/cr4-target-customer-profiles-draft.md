# CR-4: Target Customer Profiles

*Draft — February 2026*

**Purpose:** When we have to make a tradeoff in the product, who do we optimize for? These profiles answer that question. They're built from CR-1 sentiment research, [[market|market sizing]] data, and competitor analysis — not imagination. They'll be validated and refined once CR-2 survey results come in.

**Decision rule:** We're launching for Profile 1. Profile 2 is the scale market. Profile 3 is parked for later.

---

## Profile 1: The Expat Optimizer

### Who they are

Male, 36–48, working in Bangkok for 3+ years. Earns 150K–400K THB/month in tech, finance, consulting, or running his own business. Goes to a CrossFit box or boutique gym 4–5x a week. Orders supplements from iHerb every couple of months — probably magnesium, omega-3, vitamin D, maybe creatine. Tracks something: steps, sleep, macros. Has a vague sense he should be doing more for his health but doesn't have a system. His trigger was either turning 35/40, a friend getting a bad diagnosis, or noticing recovery takes longer than it used to.

He's not sick. He's annoyed that no one can tell him how to perform better.

### Current behavior

Gets an annual checkup at Bumrungrad, [[bnh-hospital|BNH]], or Samitivej — usually the 15K–25K THB executive package. Sometimes adds hormone panels at extra cost. The experience takes half a day. He sits in a nice waiting room, moves through stations, gets blood drawn, does an ECG, maybe an ultrasound. At the end, a doctor spends 8–12 minutes flipping through results, says "everything looks normal," maybe flags cholesterol or vitamin D, and sends him home with a spiral-bound PDF.

He might upload that PDF to ChatGPT later, get some generic advice, and then forget about it. He does this once a year. Between checkups, he has no system for tracking whether anything improved.

Current annual health spend: 60K–120K THB (gym membership 3K–5K/month, supplements 2K–4K/month, annual checkup 15K–25K, occasional physio or specialist visit).

### Core frustration

The gap between getting data and knowing what to do with it.

> "Many of us have done a routine health check and left still unsure what the results really meant. Most clinics give you a few tests, a simple report, and that's it." — ASEAN Now forum

> "When done, you are handed a spiffy book with your numbers... no discussion." — TripAdvisor user, describing Bumrungrad

He's also increasingly suspicious of upselling:

> "I am very distrustful of the private hospital network here in Thailand, we are customers not patients. I will question the docs respectfully on every issue that they recommend treatment for." — ASEAN Now

He doesn't distrust medicine. He distrusts the incentive structure.

### What "success" looks like

He opens the [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]] app on a Sunday morning with his coffee. He reviews the action plan from his last blood test — checks which supplements he should still be taking, re-reads the dietary guidance for his slightly elevated fasting glucose, sees that his testosterone is in a good range now compared to 6 months ago. He feels like he has a handle on his health. Not anxious, not confused — informed and in control.

When a friend mentions getting a checkup at Bumrungrad, he says "just use BodyCypher, it's way better" without being asked.

### Willingness to pay

He already spends 60K–120K THB/year on health optimization. A 3,500 THB/month membership (42K THB/year) that replaces his annual checkup AND improves his supplement routine would feel like consolidation, not a new expense. Anything under 5,000 THB/month feels reasonable. Above 7,000 THB/month, he starts comparing to premium clinic packages and expects white-glove service.

Sweet spot: 3,000–5,000 THB/month. The 3,500 THB/month target price lands well.

### Where to find them

**Offline:** CrossFit boxes (especially CrossFit BKK, CrossFit Krungthep), F45, Base Bangkok, boutique gyms in Sukhumvit/Sathorn/Silom. Running clubs (Mikkeller Running Club Bangkok, Hash House Harriers). Weekend races and obstacle course events.

**Online:** Bangkok Expats Facebook group (200K+ members), r/Thailand, r/Bangkok, ASEAN Now forums. Strava. LinkedIn (they're professionals — some will respond to thought leadership content about health optimization). Podcasts: Huberman, Attia, Rogan health episodes.

**Trust channel:** Word of mouth from other expats. This guy doesn't click Instagram ads. He signs up because his training partner or colleague told him about it.

### Risk/concern

**"Is this legit?"** He's been burned by polished health marketing before. He needs to see: a real doctor behind the product, a real lab partner, and ideally a testimonial from someone he knows or respects. The app looking too slick without substance would actually backfire — he'd assume it's a wellness scam.

**Privacy.** He's sharing blood data with a startup. He'll want to know where data is stored, who sees it, and whether it could affect insurance.

**"Will I actually use it?"** He's signed up for health apps before and abandoned them. The action plan needs to be genuinely useful on repeat visits, not just a one-time read.

## Profile 2: The Affluent Thai Professional

### Who they are

Female or male, 30–42, working at a Thai or multinational corporation in Bangkok. Earns 80K–200K THB/month. Title is something like marketing director, product manager, or runs a small business. English-proficient — consumes English-language wellness content on Instagram and YouTube, follows accounts like @drmarkhyman or Thai wellness influencers. Buys high-end supplements from GNC at Emporium or orders from Lazada/Shopee. Does yoga, Pilates, or goes to a premium gym like Virgin Active or Fitness First Platinum.

She got interested in proactive health after seeing friends her age start dealing with thyroid issues, PCOS, or burnout. She's heard about "longevity" and "biological age" but doesn't know where to start. Her parents do annual checkups at Bangkok Hospital through company insurance, and she's done the same — it feels like a checkbox, not a tool.

### Current behavior

Gets an annual checkup through corporate health insurance — typically a 5K–10K THB package at a network hospital. It covers basics: CBC, lipid panel, liver function, fasting glucose. No hormones, no performance markers, no interpretation beyond "normal range." She's never paid out of pocket for a more comprehensive panel because she doesn't know what to ask for.

She takes a daily multivitamin and maybe collagen. She's curious about personalized supplements but skeptical — there's too much marketing noise and she can't tell what's real.

She uses LINE for everything. She follows VitaLab on Instagram but hasn't signed up because 4K THB/month feels like a lot for something she's not sure about, and the brand feels very "Thai wellness influencer" — not clinical enough for her.

Current annual health spend: 30K–60K THB (gym 2K–4K/month, supplements 1K–2K/month, company-provided checkup, occasional dermatology or specialist visit).

### Core frustration

She knows she should care more about her health data but doesn't have a starting point. The company checkup is a formality. She doesn't trust the hospital's package recommendations because they feel sales-driven:

> "I know people who have gotten reeled into getting more tests done in private hospitals without really needing them. Don't want to fall into that trap." — TripAdvisor user

And the premium options feel out of reach:

> "Their recommended packages for my age group have gone up and up and up and are now in the 30-35K range, which is too much for me these days." — ASEAN Now

She wants someone to just tell her: "Here's what to test, here's what it means, here's what to do." Without the upsell, without the confusion.

### What "success" looks like

She tells her friends at brunch: "I finally understand my blood work." She shares a screenshot of her action plan in a LINE group. She switched from a generic multivitamin to 3 specific supplements that her results said she actually needed, and she noticed a difference in her energy levels. When her mom asks about her health checkup, she pulls up the app and walks her through it — her mom is impressed and asks if it's available in Thai.

### Willingness to pay

Lower price sensitivity than Profile 1 for the right product, but higher skepticism barrier. She needs social proof before committing. 

3,500 THB/month is at the upper edge of impulse — she'd try it if a friend recommended it. 2,000–3,000 THB/month would feel like an easy yes. An introductory offer or a one-time "try it" package at 5K–8K THB (single test + action plan) could convert her before she commits to a subscription.

She'd compare the price to her monthly Pilates class (3K–5K THB) or her skincare routine (2K–5K THB/month). If BodyCypher feels like it belongs in that category of "investment in myself," the price works.

### Where to find them

**Offline:** Virgin Active, Fitness First Platinum, yoga/Pilates studios in Thonglor/Ekkamai/Ari. Health food cafés (Broccoli Revolution, Veganerie). Wellness events and pop-ups.

**Online:** Instagram (primary discovery channel). LINE (primary engagement channel — this is where she'd sign up, ask questions, get support). Thai wellness influencer audiences. Facebook wellness groups (Thai language, but she reads English content too). TikTok health content.

**Trust channel:** Instagram recommendation from someone she follows, or a friend sharing their experience in a LINE group. She does NOT discover health products through Google search or Reddit. Influencer partnerships and friend referrals are the path.

### Risk/concern

**"Is this for me?"** If the branding feels too "biohacker bro" or too clinical, she'll bounce. The product needs to feel premium and approachable, not intimidating.

**Language.** She's English-proficient but emotionally more comfortable in Thai. An English-only app is fine for v1, but she'd engage more deeply with Thai-language action plans. This is a v2 consideration.

**Social proof.** She won't be the first person she knows to try something. She needs to see testimonials, Instagram content, or a friend's recommendation before she signs up. Unlike Profile 1 who might try it based on the concept alone, she needs to see proof it works.

## Profile 3: The Medical Tourist Repeater (Parked)

### Who they are

Male or female, 45–65, based in Australia, UK, Middle East, or the US. Visits Bangkok 1–2x/year — partly for vacation, partly because healthcare here is dramatically cheaper than home. Gets a comprehensive checkup each visit at Bumrungrad or Bangkok Hospital. Might combine it with dental work, a dermatology visit, or elective procedures.

### Current behavior

Spends 20K–50K THB per visit on a premium checkup package. Genuinely impressed by the facilities and speed compared to their home country. But they have no continuity between visits — different doctor each time, no one tracking their trends, no follow-up.

> "If you are happy to know that 'you'll live', the check up should be fine. But, unlike the check ups I have had in the States, there were no follow-up recommendations." — TripAdvisor user

> "Coming from the USA, I find health care in Thailand extremely inexpensive, and a fantastic value." — TripAdvisor user

### Why we're parking this

They're interesting because they have high willingness to pay and genuine unmet needs (continuity, follow-up, longitudinal tracking). But they're hard to acquire (they're not in Bangkok day-to-day), hard to retain (1–2 visits/year), and the supplement delivery logistics to international addresses adds complexity.

Worth revisiting once we have a working product and want to expand the funnel. A "Bangkok health home base" positioning could work for this segment later, potentially through partnerships with medical tourism platforms.

### Quick profile

- **Willingness to pay:** High (they already spend 20K–50K THB per visit)
- **Where to find them:** TripAdvisor medical tourism forums, medical tourism agencies, hospital referral partnerships
- **Key need:** Continuity between visits, someone who "knows their history"
- **Risk:** Low engagement frequency, international logistics

## How to Use These Profiles

### Product tradeoff decisions

| Decision | Profile 1 says | Profile 2 says | We do |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| Language at launch? | English only is fine | Prefers Thai but can use English | English only (v1) |
| App vs. PDF for v1? | Either — he cares about content quality | App signals legitimacy | App (but PDF ok for concierge MVP) |
| Hormone panels included? | Essential — this is why he'd switch | Interested but doesn't know what to ask for | Yes — it's a key differentiator |
| LINE integration? | Nice to have | Must have | Build for v2, use manually for v1 |
| Supplement recommendations? | Wants them — already buys on iHerb | Would try them if recommended | Include in action plan; delivery is upsell |
| Price sensitivity? | 3,500 THB/month is fine | Needs intro offer or friend referral | Launch at 3,500 but offer single-test trial |

### Go-to-market priority

**Launch (Q3 2026):** Profile 1. Personal network, gym communities, expat groups. Word of mouth. English only. The concierge MVP is built for this person.

**Scale (Q4 2026 – Q1 2027):** Profile 2 enters through friend referrals and Instagram. This is when brand, social proof, and influencer partnerships matter. Thai-language support becomes a priority.

**Later (2027+):** Profile 3 explored through medical tourism channel partnerships.
