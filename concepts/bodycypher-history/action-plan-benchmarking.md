# Blood test action plan benchmarking for BodyCypher

**The best blood test interpretation platforms today combine personalized optimal ranges, AI-driven prioritization, and physician oversight to deliver actionable health plans  -  but none does everything well.** After benchmarking 10 leading companies, a clear pattern emerges: the market splits between breadth-of-testing plays (Function Health, Mito Health) and depth-of-interpretation plays (InsideTracker, SelfDecode), with a massive gap at the intersection. The most consistent user complaint across every platform  -  and BodyCypher's clearest opportunity  -  is that AI-generated recommendations feel generic. One Reddit user's assessment of Function Health captured the sentiment perfectly: *"It is so AI generated it's offensive. Nothing personal or bespoke about the write up."* This research covers deliverables, structure, supplement pipelines, user sentiment, and biomarker education across the competitive landscape to give [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]] a concrete benchmark for its Thailand launch.

---

## 1. What leading companies actually deliver

### InsideTracker sets the dashboard standard

InsideTracker's web dashboard is the most frequently praised interface in the space. It organizes **48 biomarkers** into 10 healthspan category scores (0-100) across cognition, fitness, endurance, inflammation, gut health, and heart health. The core visual is a three-zone color-coded graph for each biomarker  -  red (at risk), yellow (needs improvement), green (optimized)  -  plotted against **personalized optimal zones** calculated from age, sex, ethnicity, activity level, and goals. This three-tier visual is universally understood by users and represents the UX gold standard.

The action plan system is the deepest in the market. Users select 2 goals from 14 options (fat loss, energy, muscle gain, fight aging, stress reduction, endurance). An algorithm called SegterraX then selects from **7,500+ recommendations** ranked by individual impact score, drawing from 2,500+ peer-reviewed publications. Recommendations include 5 "Goal Foods" matched from an 8,000-food database, specific supplement protocols with dosing, exercise modifications, and lifestyle changes. Each recommendation card has an impact score (1-10) and a check-in feature for habit tracking. Weekly "Healthspan Habit Reports" deliver one new personalized habit per week.

**InnerAge 2.0**, their biological age score, uses the Klemera-Doubal methodology combined with their proprietary dataset. It analyzes 17 biomarkers for males, 13 for females (glucose, LDL, triglycerides, HbA1c, GGT, among others), weighted by correlation strength with aging. Users report that **60% reduce their InnerAge** on follow-up  -  a powerful engagement metric. The tone is coaching-oriented and encouraging ("You're almost done!", "Amazing, isn't it?"). Pricing runs $199/year for lab uploads up to $1,781/year for full testing packages. Format is web dashboard plus mobile app  -  no PDFs.

Key user feedback: *"Easy to read and understand... mixed scientific data with actionable recommendations"* and *"stuffed with important information but easy to navigate thanks to great graphic design."* The main criticism from expert reviewers: recommendations treat biomarkers too independently and don't elegantly connect data points across markers.

### Function Health wins on breadth, clinician notes are the crown jewel

Function Health tests **100+ biomarkers** in a single draw  -  the broadest panel in DTC testing  -  spanning autoimmunity, environmental toxins, heart, heavy metals, metabolic health, hormones, thyroid, and cancer detection. Co-founded by Dr. Mark Hyman, it's positioned as preventive medicine.

The dashboard displays biomarkers on a binary in-range/out-of-range scale, which multiple reviewers criticize as lacking nuance. But the **clinician notes**  -  physician-written summaries delivered 3-4 weeks after blood draw  -  are consistently rated as worth the $499/year membership alone. One user reported a 415-word personalized summary with cross-biomarker analysis connecting thyroid and cholesterol markers, specific dietary recommendations, and supplement suggestions. Users describe these notes as *"the type of information and advice I always wanted to get from my doctors, but never did"* and *"more in-depth analysis than I have received from any doctor."*

The critical UX problem: results trickle in over 7-10 days before clinician notes arrive, causing anxiety. Users Google unfamiliar biomarkers and catastrophize. The reviewer from Gene Food noted the platform's *"digital bedside manner could use some work."* Format is web-only, no dedicated mobile app.

### Mito Health offers the best hybrid AI-plus-doctor model

Singapore-founded Mito Health (now US-focused, Y Combinator-backed) delivers what may be the most balanced approach at **$399/year**. Their dashboard organizes biomarkers by body system rather than individual markers, with an organ-system health status view showing which systems are at highest risk. The standout feature is **risk horizon assessments** projecting health outcomes over 10, 20, and 30 years  -  a forward-looking differentiator no other platform offers.

The action plan spans five pillars (nutrition, exercise, sleep, supplementation, stress) with each recommendation including medical rationale, implementation steps, and CMO tips. A **15-minute clinician consultation** is included. Their **MitoMD AI chatbot** (beta) is trained exclusively on the user's biomarker data, family history, and Mito's internal research database  -  deliberately not scraping external web sources to avoid hallucination. Users report: *"The app is great and gives you a lot of info in a manageable way and then the action plan and doctor's analysis is awesome. Very easy to understand and follow up."*

### Superpower leads on price, but supplement upselling draws criticism

At **$199/year**, [[superpower|Superpower]] is the cheapest comprehensive option. It tests 100+ biomarkers (though roughly 30% are calculated ratios, not direct lab tests) across 17 health categories with a gamified **Superpower Score** (0-100). Biological age uses a modified PhenoAge algorithm from 9 blood biomarkers.

The controversial differentiator is an integrated **supplement marketplace** with 20% member discounts. AI recommendations routinely suggest **$400+/month** in supplements, creating a conflict of interest that sophisticated users immediately flag. The Superpower brand suffered a serious trust breach when the community discovered they used doctored screenshots of r/Biohackers Reddit posts in Meta ads  -  reaction: *"Never using them. Red flags galore."* The tone is aspirational and consumer-tech; a reviewer noted the AI *"tells you what is out of range but doesn't do a great job explaining why it matters or how different biomarkers relate to each other."*

### Bioniq turns blood tests into custom supplements

Bioniq is unique because the primary deliverable is a physical product  -  **personalized supplement granules** with up to 120 ingredients selected from 10 million+ formula combinations. Their patent-pending algorithm analyzes blood markers plus BMI, weight, height, health questionnaire, and goals against a database of **6 million+ biochemical data points**. The Swiss-manufactured granules use microencapsulation for 24-hour sustained release. The app tracks biomarkers over time and shows the formula recipe. At **$199/month** for the blood-test-based PRO tier, it's the most expensive ongoing commitment. Cristiano Ronaldo is both investor and endorser. The model works because users don't need to interpret results themselves  -  they just take their granules.

### Remaining five companies fill specific niches

**Everlab** (Australia, $799-2,999 AUD/year) provides the most comprehensive clinical model with doctors, nutritionists, physiotherapists, and coaches collaborating on unified plans. Their "digital health avatar" consolidates 20 years of medical history. **SelfDecode** ($99-319/year) is the only platform combining DNA analysis (200 million genetic variants) with lab results, using polygenic risk scores to contextualize blood markers with genetic predispositions  -  though users report the intersection can overwhelm. **Marek Health** is entirely human-driven with no dashboard, delivering results via email and Zoom consultations, popular in the hormone optimization and TRT community. **Kantesti** (Germany) is the purest AI play  -  upload a blood test PDF, receive a 40-45 page report in 60 seconds for ~$4.49, with CE medical device marking and 75+ language support. **Healthmatters.io** ($79/year or $250 lifetime) is purely a data organization and biomarker education tool with 10,000+ markers but zero personalized recommendations.

## 2. The anatomy of a best-in-class blood test action plan

### Sections that every competitive platform includes

Across all 10 companies, the baseline structure contains: an **executive summary** with overall health scoring (InsideTracker's 10 category scores, Superpower's 17-category system, or Kantesti's single 0-100 score), **per-marker breakdowns** with visual range indicators, **dietary recommendations** (eat more/eat less food lists), **supplement recommendations** with specific dosing, and **lifestyle modification** guidance covering exercise, sleep, and stress.

The critical difference between mediocre and best-in-class reports lies in six additional components. First, **biological age calculation**  -  InsideTracker uses InnerAge 2.0 (Klemera-Doubal method, 13-17 biomarkers), Superpower uses PhenoAge (9 biomarkers), and Mito Health uses a proprietary formula. These are blood-based calculations, not epigenetic methylation clocks, and serve primarily as engagement features. A scientist reviewer noted biological age is a *"superb engagement feature"* because it gamifies health optimization with a single trackable number.

Second, **priority ranking of interventions**  -  InsideTracker leads here, ordering every recommendation by an impact score that weights both the severity of biomarker deviation and the strength of scientific evidence behind the intervention. They advise users to start with just 3-5 new habits. Most competitors rank only by biomarker severity (how far out of range), ignoring evidence quality.

Third, **trend visualization** across tests over time. Every top platform supports this, but the key UX insight is the ability to overlay interventions (what you changed) with biomarker changes. InsideTracker's trend lines change color as they cross between zones. Fourth, **risk projections**  -  Mito Health's 10/20/30-year risk horizon is unique and creates urgency without panic. Fifth, **food specificity**  -  InsideTracker recommends particular foods from an 8,000-food database (e.g., "eat fatty fish like snapper, salmon, and whitefish twice a week"), while Viome categorizes 370+ individual foods into four tiers with specific explanations for each. Most competitors stay at the generic "eat more leafy greens" level. Sixth, **follow-up testing schedules**  -  the standard recommendation is retesting every 3-6 months, with Mito Health uniquely recommending selective retesting of only the markers your care team flags.

### Features the best platforms include that most skip

**Confidence levels on recommendations** represent the industry's biggest gap. No platform found explicitly labels recommendations with evidence grades (e.g., "Level A evidence" vs. "Level C"). InsideTracker's impact score implicitly reflects evidence strength, but the grading is opaque. Fullscript's Evidence-Based Decision Support framework ties protocols to evidence levels for practitioners, but this doesn't reach consumers. This is a clear differentiation opportunity for BodyCypher.

**Exercise prescriptions** are the weakest component across all platforms. Most stick to general guidance ("walk 30 min after lunch 4x/week") rather than programming specifics. InsideTracker gets closest with categories like "Strength and Power" and "Endurance" personalized to activity level, but nothing approaches a proper training plan. **Wearable data integration** varies dramatically  -  InsideTracker integrates well with Apple Health, Oura, Fitbit, and Garmin to generate daily "ProTips," while Superpower's Oura/WHOOP integration was described as *"woefully incomplete"* by reviewers. **Goal-based personalization** (longevity vs. performance vs. weight loss) is handled best by InsideTracker's 13+ goal options that filter the entire recommendation set.

## 3. How companies connect blood results to supplement recommendations

### The technical logic chain from biomarker to bottle

The core pipeline across the industry follows an eight-step logic chain: **Measure** (capture biomarker value) → **Classify** (compare against standard, optimal, and personalized ranges) → **Contextualize** (factor in demographics, medications, supplements, lifestyle, goals, and optionally DNA) → **Match** (rule-based or ML-based matching against an intervention database) → **Prioritize** (rank by deviation severity, evidence strength, number of markers addressed, and user goals) → **Safety check** (screen for drug-supplement interactions and max daily allowances) → **Personalize dose** (adjust by body weight, height, BMI, current intake, and absorption genetics) → **Deliver and iterate** (implement, retest in 3-6 months, adjust).

InsideTracker uses what they describe as a *"rule-based algorithmic expert system"*  -  their SegterraR engine mines data from 180,000+ healthy individuals to find similar profiles, then SegterraX generates recommendations from their curated database. Critically, they note that *"the recommendations you see are just as important as those you don't see"*  -  filtering out inappropriate recommendations is a core algorithmic function. When multiple markers are out of range, their impact scoring surfaces recommendations that address the most biomarkers simultaneously.

### Fullscript keeps the practitioner in the loop

Fullscript operates as a **B2B platform** used by 125,000+ healthcare providers  -  it's not direct-to-consumer. The workflow: practitioners order labs through integrated EHR partners (Practice Better, Healthie, PracticeQ), results return to the practitioner, and they build supplement plans using Fullscript's tools. **Fullscript Assist**, their AI layer, provides clinical decision support by auto-flagging drug-supplement interactions using the NatMed database (updated daily), surfacing nutrient depletion alerts, and offering side-by-side product comparisons. They also provide **160+ evidence-based protocol templates** (e.g., "Metabolic Health Blood Sugar Support") created by their Integrative Medical Advisory Team. The key distinction: Fullscript does not automatically generate protocols from lab data. The practitioner remains the decision-maker. This "practitioner-in-the-loop" model is the safest approach and the one most defensible with a doctor co-founder.

### Bioniq's custom compounding is the most vertically integrated model

Bioniq's pipeline is fully closed-loop. Their algorithm processes blood biomarker data plus health questionnaire, BMI, and lifestyle data against **6 million+ biochemical data points** from customers across five continents. It generates a unique formula from **10 million+ possible combinations**, selecting up to 120 ingredients (vitamins, minerals, amino acids, phytonutrients, antioxidants, prebiotic fiber). The formula is manufactured in their Swiss pharmaceutical facility using patented microencapsulated granules that resist stomach acid for optimal small-intestine absorption. A nutritionist consultation validates the formula. Retesting every 6 months triggers formula evolution. Their study of 403 members showed improved nutrient levels in those with deficiencies while maintaining optimal levels in balanced users.

### Superpower's marketplace approach creates trust problems

Superpower's pipeline runs from onboarding data (lifestyle, nutrition, clinical history, goals) through their SuperpowerAI LLM, which draws from medical literature and proprietary data to generate supplement and lifestyle recommendations. The AI reportedly follows *"structured clinical logic"* and *"escalates to human review when needed."* Recommendations link directly to their marketplace with member discounts. The fundamental trust problem: when the platform recommending supplements is also selling them, users question whether the AI optimizes for their health or for revenue. Reviewers consistently flag that the AI suggests **$400+/month** in products. For BodyCypher, this is a cautionary tale  -  if you build a supplement pipeline, the recommendation engine must be architecturally separated from commerce to maintain trust.

### Other models worth studying

**Rootine** uses a three-data-layer approach (DNA analyzing 50+ gene variants for nutrient absorption, blood tests for current levels, and lifestyle quiz) to generate custom vitamin microbeads with precision dosing to the milligram. Their "Evergreen Algorithm" is backed by 400+ peer-reviewed studies. **Viome** takes a unique path through mRNA analysis (what microbes are doing, not just present) to generate 50+ health scores and custom supplement capsules. **Thorne**, primarily a supplement manufacturer (NSF certified, Mayo Clinic research partner), added testing  -  their at-home tests generate algorithm-driven recommendations for specific Thorne products, keeping the brand ecosystem closed.

The key technical challenge across all pipelines: **training data bias**. Most studies focus on clinically sick populations. InsideTracker specifically addressed this by building their reference database from their own health-conscious users  -  a methodological choice BodyCypher should consider for the Thai market, where population-level biomarker distributions may differ from Western datasets.

## 4. What users actually think about AI blood test interpretation

### The "so AI generated it's offensive" problem

The single most actionable finding from community research is that knowledgeable users have developed an acute sensitivity to generic AI output. The Reddit quote about Function Health  -  *"it is so AI generated it's offensive. Nothing personal or bespoke about the write up"*  -  captures a sentiment repeated across platforms. Another Function Health user on Trustpilot: *"I liked the composite of tests (not your analysis). I did not like the AI based dialogue."*

The expert Gene Food reviewer articulated what power users want: *"What I am looking for from products like this... is how smart the app is, meaning how many data points are being elegantly and meaningfully strung together?"* This reviewer found InsideTracker's gut health and heart health recommendations were identical despite different biomarker profiles, and noted that a recommendation for yogurt to lower LDL-C in one phenotype could actually increase LDL-C and ApoB  -  a nuance the AI missed entirely.

A WellnessPulse study scored general-purpose AI models (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) at **1-5 out of 10** for blood test interpretation, with ChatGPT scoring 2/10 on a lipid panel case. This establishes a low bar that purpose-built systems must clear convincingly.

### What builds trust versus what destroys it

Users trust AI interpretation when they see **specific citations** (InsideTracker praised for *"links to studies backing up almost every claim"*), **personalized optimal ranges** based on demographics (*"a hard-charging triathlete will have an 'optimal' ferritin that is higher than a couch potato"*), **longitudinal trend data** that shows change over time, and **CLIA/CAP certified lab partners** like Quest Diagnostics.

Trust collapses when users encounter **black-box algorithms** where the reasoning isn't visible (InsideTracker's DNA insights criticized as a *"black box"* because SNPs forming risk scores aren't disclosed), **supplement upselling** that creates revenue-aligned recommendations, **astroturfing** (Superpower's doctored Reddit post scandal), and **auto-renewal billing** (a persistent SelfDecode complaint). The Substack PhD reviewer also flagged **data privacy** as an emerging trust factor: *"Direct-to-consumer health companies operate under fewer legal restrictions on data ownership, sharing, and privacy... 'de-identified data' can still be shared and even become a company asset in the event of a merger."*

### The features users explicitly ask for

Five requests surface repeatedly across communities. First, **real physician review**  -  this is the number-one gap. Users want a human doctor to contextualize results, not replace AI but validate it. Empirical Health's inclusion of an MD review for $190 is specifically cited as a competitive advantage. Second, **cross-biomarker intelligence**  -  connecting markers to each other rather than analyzing each in isolation. Third, **good digital bedside manner**  -  not releasing alarming out-of-range results without context, managing anxiety proactively. Fourth, **prescriptions and referrals** when warranted, not just supplements. Fifth, **fast turnaround**  -  InsideTracker's 5-day results are praised versus Function Health's 10-12 day drip plus up to 3-month waits for clinician notes.

For BodyCypher launching in Thailand, these findings suggest the action plan should lead with physician-validated interpretation, explicitly show the AI's reasoning chain for each recommendation, and avoid any appearance of supplement revenue alignment.

## 5. How platforms teach users about individual biomarkers

### Healthmatters.io has breadth, InsideTracker has design

Healthmatters.io's **10,000+ biomarker database** is the industry's most comprehensive reference. Each marker page follows a consistent template: marker name and panel, prominently displayed optimal range with a checkmark icon, multi-paragraph description of biological function and clinical significance, "Why is this test important?" section, separate "What does it mean if your result is too low/high?" sections with numbered potential causes and recommended follow-up tests. The tone is semi-clinical and encyclopedic  -  aimed at health-literate consumers and practitioners. A unique feature: an **"Edit Range" button** allowing users to customize their own reference ranges based on their goals or practitioner guidance. The limitation is that it's text-heavy with no personalized recommendations and minimal visual engagement.

InsideTracker's approach is the opposite  -  focused visual design over textual depth. The three-zone color graph (red/yellow/green) is the centerpiece of every marker view. Trend lines change color as values move between zones across test dates. Crucially, InsideTracker **won't test a biomarker unless there's evidence it can be improved through diet, supplements, exercise, or lifestyle**  -  a philosophical choice that keeps every marker actionable. Their marker pages include food imagery (actual food photos, not icons), which multiple reviewers praise for making dietary recommendations tangible.

Function Health's binary in-range/out-of-range display is its weakest UX element. Reviewers specifically requested a **third "borderline" tier** to avoid false alarms from barely out-of-range results. The generic marker descriptions read like boilerplate until clinician notes arrive weeks later.

SelfDecode uniquely **cross-references each lab marker with genetic data**  -  if your glucose is borderline, the system checks your diabetes-related SNPs to contextualize the risk. Each marker shows both standard reference ranges and functional/optimal ranges side by side. Their "SelfHacked Method" combines literature review, clinical experience, and longevity research from Dr. Michael Lustgarten to derive optimal targets.

### Optimal ranges versus reference ranges  -  the key differentiator

This distinction is perhaps the single most important product decision for BodyCypher. Standard lab reference ranges represent the **central 95% of a lab's testing population**  -  designed to detect disease, not optimize health. As a PMC article noted, in a panel of 20 tests, there's a **38% probability** that at least one result will be "abnormal" in a perfectly healthy person. Worse, as population health declines (obesity rates rose from 23% to 42.4% since 1962, testosterone has been steadily declining), the "normal" range shifts to include increasingly unhealthy baselines.

Each platform handles this differently. InsideTracker calculates personalized optimal zones from their database of hundreds of thousands of users segmented by age, sex, ethnicity, and activity level. Function Health uses doctor-curated optimal ranges from their advisory board. SelfDecode uses functional medicine ranges. Healthmatters.io lets users set their own ranges.

**There is no universal scientific consensus** on optimal ranges for most markers. The concept is primarily championed by functional and integrative medicine practitioners. However, peer-reviewed evidence supports narrower targets for certain well-studied markers: vitamin D at **40-60 ng/mL** optimal versus the standard 20-50; triglycerides at **below 88-90 mg/dL** versus the standard below 150; TSH at **0.5-2.0 mIU/L** versus the standard 0.45-4.5; ferritin in athletic women at **above 40 ng/dL** versus the standard above 12.

For BodyCypher's Thai market, this creates both an opportunity and a responsibility. Thai population biomarker distributions may differ from the Western datasets underlying most platforms' optimal ranges. Building Thailand-specific optimal ranges  -  even starting with published Southeast Asian population data  -  would be a genuine differentiator and a strong talking point with a doctor co-founder.

### The UX that works for non-medical users

Research on health literacy reveals that **layered information architecture** is essential: summary first, then detail, then deep dive. InsideTracker's approach (category score → marker status → specific recommendations) outperforms encyclopedic dumps. The universal finding across PMC studies is that standard tabular lab reports (Quest/Labcorp style) are designed for clinicians, not patients. Eight specific competencies for "laboratory literacy" have been identified as necessary to interpret results  -  most consumers lack several.

The practical implications for BodyCypher: use **color-coded visual range indicators** as the primary communication layer (universally understood), provide **"Why this matters to you"** personalized explanations rather than generic "What is ferritin" educational content, enable **trend tracking** as the primary longitudinal engagement mechanism, and implement **progressive disclosure** so users aren't overwhelmed with all data simultaneously. Food imagery (actual food photos, not icons) meaningfully increases engagement with dietary recommendations.

## Conclusion: What BodyCypher should build

The competitive landscape reveals five strategic insights for BodyCypher's action plan format. First, **physician validation is non-negotiable**  -  the market is splitting between pure-AI plays that users increasingly distrust and physician-backed services that command premium trust. Having a doctor co-founder who validates the interpretation layer is a genuine competitive advantage, not just credibility theater.

Second, **cross-biomarker intelligence is the frontier**  -  every platform analyzes markers largely in isolation, and expert users consistently identify this as the biggest quality gap. Building an AI layer that explains how markers relate to each other (e.g., connecting thyroid function to cholesterol to energy) would immediately differentiate BodyCypher from all 10 competitors studied.

Third, **the supplement pipeline must be architecturally separated from commerce**  -  Superpower's trust erosion from marketplace upselling is a clear warning. If BodyCypher monetizes supplements, the recommendation engine should be provably independent from revenue incentives. Fullscript's practitioner-in-the-loop model offers the most defensible template.

Fourth, **Thailand-specific optimal ranges represent untapped value**  -  no Western platform has Southeast Asian population-calibrated ranges. Even basic adjustments for Thai dietary patterns, genetic markers like thalassemia prevalence, and tropical-climate vitamin D dynamics would create a moat that global competitors cannot easily replicate.

Fifth, **the report tone should target "doctor who actually has time for you"**  -  not clinical enough to intimidate, not casual enough to undermine trust. Function Health's clinician notes hit this sweet spot when they arrive. Mito Health's MitoMD chatbot approach  -  trained on the user's own data, available 24/7  -  represents the most promising model for scaling that tone. The format should be a web dashboard with mobile app access, downloadable PDFs for doctor sharing, and an AI chat layer for ongoing questions. This combination covers every use case identified in user research.
