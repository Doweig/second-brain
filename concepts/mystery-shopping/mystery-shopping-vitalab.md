# Mystery Shopping Evaluation — VitaLab Thailand

*Date of visit: January 22, 2026 (blood draw at home)*
*Package purchased: Blood Test + Men Total Care supplements — ฿21,500 first month*
*Clinic tier: Accessible Premium (direct competitor)*

---

## 1. Pre-Visit Experience

**How did you book?** LINE (@vitalab.th)

**Booking friction:** 3/5
- Multiple back-and-forth interactions on LINE — conversation thread was very long
- Pricing was transparent — they laid out all packages and tiers clearly
- Yes, they upsold: pushed 3-month (10% blood test discount) and 6-month (20% blood test discount) commitments. Supplements stay full price regardless — smart tactic to lock in supplement revenue at maximum margin
- First attempted to book January 15 but earliest nurse availability was January 22 — **over a week wait**, which felt excessive. Psychological friction: you're motivated to start, and then you wait

**Pre-visit communication:**
- Sent prep instructions via LINE (fasting required)
- Sent a lengthy health questionnaire to fill out beforehand (5–10 minutes)
- Communication was frequent and thorough, if a bit spammy — they also added me to a LINE group
- English was okay, not great — serviceable but not polished (3/5)

---

## 2. Arrival & Check-in

**Time:** Appointment at 9:00 AM. Arrived late (~9:45 AM). Nurse said it was fine — no other patients that day.

**First impression:** N/A — nurse came to home. VitaLab has a physical clinic at RainHill (Thonglor) but it opens at 11 AM, which is too late for fasting blood draws. Most clients use the home nurse service, which appears to be free/included.

**Check-in process:**
- Nurse arrived with a kit: blood pressure monitor, blood oxygen monitor, syringes, vials
- Had a paper form to sign
- Professional setup, quick registration
- English proficiency of nurse: 2/5 (so-so, but functional for the task)
- No confirmation of which specific tests were being run

## 3. Testing Process

**Total stations/stops:** 1 (home blood draw only)

**Flow description:** Blood pressure, blood oxygen, then blood draw. Simple, fast, efficient.

| Station | Wait Time | Duration | Staff Friendliness (1-5) | Notes |
|---------|-----------|----------|--------------------------|-------|
| Vitals (BP, SpO2) | 0 min | ~2 min | 4 | Quick and professional |
| Blood draw | 0 min | ~5 min | 4 | Very smooth, painless |

**Blood draw experience:**
- Very professional. Painless. Fast — "boom boom boom"
- Did not explain what was being tested
- **7 vials drawn**
- First time doing at-home blood draw — genuinely impressed by the convenience

**Assembly line feeling?** 1/5 — Very personal. One-on-one at home. This was the highlight of the entire experience.

**Intel from nurse (asked casually):**
- ~2 patients per week (low volume — concerning for business viability)
- Patients mostly female, mostly Thai, around 40s age range

---

## 4. Doctor Consultation

**Wait time before seeing doctor:** Results took ~2 weeks to arrive. Then scheduled a phone call via LINE — available slot was a Tuesday at 2 PM.

**Consultation duration:** ~5 minutes

**Doctor's approach:**
- Dr. Ploylada called directly — phone consultation, no video
- She asked me to open the PDF and then rapid-fired through the results
- She jumped between pages without indicating which page/section she was on — impossible to follow along
- No explanation of what numbers meant — just "this is good, this is not good, this is high"
- No actionable recommendations beyond "I'm going to send you supplements"
- Did not ask about lifestyle, goals, or concerns
- **Felt extremely rushed** — one of the worst consultations I've ever had
- English accent made comprehension difficult — understood maybe half of what she said
- When asked what supplements would be included: "You will see in the package, there will be a list"

**Upselling:**
- No additional tests recommended during consultation
- Supplements were the assumed next step — already purchased as part of the package
- No discussion of whether supplements were actually needed based on results

**Questions asked and quality of answers:**
1. "What supplements will be in my package?" → "You will see in the package, there will be a list." (Deflected)
2. Tried to ask follow-up questions about specific values → She had already moved on or hung up

## 5. Results Delivery

**When did you receive results?** ~2 weeks after blood draw (slow)

**Format:** PDF sent via LINE

**Quality of the report:**
- Number of biomarkers included: **42 biomarkers**
- Reference ranges included: Yes
- Results flagged (high/low): Yes
- Written interpretation: Minimal — some color-coded vitamin ranges (rainbow: red → yellow → green) which was slightly better than a raw lab report, but only just
- Action plan or recommendations section: **None**

**The report was clearly an N Health lab report with the logo cropped out.** The PDF had "Lulana Clinic" branding stamped on it (another clinic also run by Dr. Ploylada). They literally just slapped their logo on a standard lab PDF. No proprietary analysis, no added interpretation layer.

**What was MISSING from the report:**
- Any written interpretation beyond color-coded ranges
- Actionable recommendations tied to specific results
- Context for what markers mean or why they matter
- Trend tracking or comparison to previous results
- Executive summary or prioritized findings

## 6. Follow-Up

**Did they schedule or suggest a follow-up?** Not explicitly. The subscription model implies ongoing monthly supplement delivery with periodic retesting (pushed via the 3/6-month discount packages).

**Did they provide any way to ask questions after the visit?** LINE remained available, but no proactive follow-up after the consultation.

**Did they offer ongoing care or just a one-time transaction?** Subscription model = ongoing supplement delivery. But the "care" component (doctor involvement) felt like a one-time, check-the-box phone call.

## 8. The BodyCypher Lens

**What would [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]] do better here?**
- Structured, written action plan instead of a rushed 5-minute phone dump — this is the entire thesis validated in real life
- Per-marker education: click on any result and understand what it means, why it matters, how to improve it
- A real consultation: 30+ minutes, video call, doctor who actually walks through findings at a pace the patient can follow
- Transparent supplement rationale: explain *why* each supplement is recommended based on *which* specific results

**What should BodyCypher steal/copy from this experience?**
- **Home blood draw service** — this was genuinely excellent. The convenience was transformative. "I can just stay home, she takes my blood, I eat breakfast." This should be the default BodyCypher experience, not clinic visits
- **Supplement packaging** — VitaLab's two-box system (morning/evening) with clear labeling was well-done. The physical unboxing experience was the one premium-feeling touchpoint
- **Health questionnaire before blood draw** — good practice for personalization context. BodyCypher's intake questionnaire serves the same purpose
- **Multi-month commitment pricing** — discounting blood tests for longer supplement commitments is a smart retention mechanic

**If this person were handed a BodyCypher action plan after this visit, what would they value most?**
- Actually understanding what their 42 biomarkers mean and what to do about the flagged ones — the thing they paid ฿21,500 for and didn't get

**Key quote or moment that captures the experience:**
> "Even ChatGPT would be better — really. Just do it yourself. Which is a rough experience, but it would be better in this case. That's how bad this was."

## 9. Business Intelligence Notes

**Company history:** VitaLab appears to be a successor to "Vita Boost" — a previous company with three doctor co-founders (2 male, 1 female). Vita Boost appears to have had financial difficulties. Dr. Ploylada (the female co-founder) then launched VitaLab as a new entity, seemingly salvaging the concept. She also runs Lulana Clinic separately.

**Lab infrastructure:** Using N Health for blood analysis (confirmed by lab report formatting). This validates the N Health partnership model — even competitors use them.

**Volume signals:** Nurse reporting ~2 patients per week suggests very low volume. Combined with the company history (Vita Boost → VitaLab pivot), this raises questions about business viability and scale.

**Target demographic (per nurse):** Mostly Thai women in their 40s. Not the expat/male demographic BodyCypher is targeting — suggesting limited overlap in practice even though the concept is similar.
