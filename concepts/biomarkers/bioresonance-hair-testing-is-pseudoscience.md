# Bioresonance hair testing is pseudoscience, not competition

**Bioresonance hair testing has zero scientific validity for detecting nutrient deficiencies, food sensitivities, or health imbalances.** Every well-designed double-blind study has shown it performs no better than random chance. Every major medical and scientific organization worldwide—including the FDA, NHS, EAACI, AAAAI, and the Swiss Society for Allergology—has explicitly rejected it. The specific company in question, Vitals (vitals.co.th), operates a textbook supplement sales funnel built on pseudoscientific testing, and poses no legitimate competitive threat to blood-based health optimization. For [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]]'s product strategy, bioresonance hair testing should be understood as a cautionary example of what to differentiate against, not a model to emulate or complement.

---

## What bioresonance actually is—and why it fails

Bioresonance originated in 1977 when German physician Franz Morell and engineer Erich Rasche developed "MORA therapy," built on the unproven premise that cells emit unique electromagnetic oscillations, that disease produces "distorted" frequencies, and that machines can read and correct these signals. The concept descends directly from **radionics**, a pseudoscience created by Albert Abrams (1864–1924), whom the American Medical Association called the "dean of gadget quacks."

The theoretical foundation collapses under scrutiny. Physicists have demonstrated that bioresonance literature is "full of systematic errors and controversies," according to a formal statement from the Swiss Society for Allergology and Immunology. A **1997 biomedical engineering analysis** proved that placing glass vials of allergens into a bioresonance device's input well does not affect device readings—because glass is not an electrical conductor. Hair, the supposed diagnostic medium, is composed primarily of keratin and is **biologically inert** once it exits the follicle. It lacks blood supply, active immune cells, and does not emit diagnostically meaningful electromagnetic frequencies.

The evidence base is unambiguous. The **Ludwig Boltzmann Institute's systematic review** (2009)—the most comprehensive to date—examined 57 references and found only 6 controlled studies meeting inclusion criteria. Conclusion: results showed "big placebo effects and big variances between patients." The landmark **Lewith et al. (2001) study in the BMJ** tested 30 volunteers with three different operators using electrodermal bioresonance and found results "did not correlate" with validated skin prick tests. No operator outperformed any other. **Semizzi et al. (2002)** found "wide variability of measurements irrespective of allergy status." **Kofler et al. (1996)** found bioresonance diagnosis matched conventional allergy testing in only **22% of cases**—worse than a coin flip for multi-category testing.

The scientific consensus is not ambiguous or evolving. It is settled and unanimous:

## Legitimate hair analysis vs. bioresonance hair testing are entirely different things

A critical distinction exists between analytical chemistry-based hair testing and bioresonance-based hair testing. They share the word "hair" and nothing else.

**Legitimate hair analysis** uses techniques like ICP-MS (Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry) to physically destroy hair via acid digestion, then measure actual elemental concentrations at parts-per-billion sensitivity. The **U.S. EPA reviewed 400+ studies** in 1979 and concluded hair is "a meaningful and representative tissue for biological monitoring for most of the toxic metals." Hair drug testing (detecting cocaine, opioids, cannabis metabolites via LC-MS/MS) is forensically validated and used by the FBI. These methods work because drugs and metals are **physically incorporated into the keratin matrix** during hair growth—actual molecules are being measured by actual instruments.

**Bioresonance hair testing** places an unprocessed hair sample on a device's input plate and claims to read "energetic frequencies." Nothing is chemically analyzed. No molecules are measured. When the Daily Mirror sent identical hair samples under two different names to Check My Body Health (a major bioresonance testing company), the results "differed so much that the reporter concluded the results were like sticking pins at random into a food chart." In a German study, bioresonance machines **could not distinguish between a wet towel and a human being**, with standard deviations exceeding 200% for repeated tests.

Even legitimate hair mineral analysis has significant limitations. A **2001 JAMA study** found that mineral levels "varied considerably between identical samples sent to the same laboratory." The AMA has opposed clinical use of hair mineral analysis due to its "unproven status and potential for healthcare fraud." The scientific consensus is captured by a PubMed publication titled simply: **"Hair analyses: worthless for vitamins, limited for minerals."**

## The Vitals-specific claims range from implausible to absurd

Evaluating the specific biomarkers Vitals claims to detect from hair via bioresonance reveals claims that are not merely unvalidated but physically impossible.

**Lycopene** is a lipophilic carotenoid measured via HPLC from blood serum. No published research exists on detecting lycopene from hair. It circulates in blood lipoproteins and has no established mechanism for incorporation into hair keratin. **DPA (Docosapentaenoic Acid)** is an omega-3 fatty acid measured from red blood cell membranes via gas chromatography. Hair is not listed among valid matrices for fatty acid analysis in any scientific review. **Phenylacetylglutamine (PAGln)** is a cutting-edge gut microbiome metabolite requiring **stable-isotope-dilution LC-MS/MS** for measurement—equipment costing hundreds of thousands of dollars. No research has identified PAGln as hair-detectable.

**Lactate** is perhaps the most revealing claim. Blood lactate is an acute metabolic marker that changes within seconds during exercise. It is a transient, water-soluble metabolite measured from fresh blood in 15–60 seconds. The idea that a static hair sample could reflect lactate levels contradicts basic biochemistry. And the claim of detecting **"Abs Muscles" strain** from hair defies all known biology—muscle strain is an acute, localized mechanical event diagnosed by physical examination or MRI. No conceivable mechanism connects specific muscle group injury to hair electromagnetic properties.

The claim of testing **400+ biomarkers** from a single hair sample is orders of magnitude beyond what any validated technology can deliver. Even cutting-edge hair metabolomics research using the most advanced mass spectrometry has identified dozens—not hundreds—of detectable compounds, and none through bioresonance.

## Regulators worldwide have taken enforcement action

The regulatory landscape reinforces the scientific verdict. The **U.S. FDA** classifies electrodiagnostic devices as **Class III** (highest risk), requiring pre-market approval that no bioresonance device has obtained. The FDA has issued warning letters to ONDAMED Inc. (2015), RoyalVibe Health Ltd. (2022), and ZYTO Technologies (2015), and has **banned importation** of electrodermal testing devices. The FTC has sued practitioners making "unsubstantiated and potentially harmful" claims.

**Australia delivered the most decisive action**: in 2019, the TGA cancelled all bioresonance devices from the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods. Expert opinions solicited by the TGA included Professor Forgas calling BICOM "the worst kind of psychological manipulation" and Professor Edzard Ernst calling bioresonance "not biologically plausible, not of proven effectiveness, potentially harmful."

The sole regulatory outlier is the **BICOM optima's CE Class IIa marking** in the EU, limited to "treatment of mild to moderate allergic diseases"—not diagnostics. This reflects the EU's lower evidence threshold compared to the FDA and does not constitute validation of the diagnostic claims made by hair testing companies. Germany, where bioresonance was invented, does not cover it under statutory health insurance.

Vitals' claim of **ISO certification** is misleading by implication. ISO 9001 certifies that business processes are consistently managed—it says nothing about whether the testing methodology produces scientifically meaningful results. A company can be ISO 9001-certified and still produce entirely meaningless test results. True methodology validation would require ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation for the specific test method plus published peer-reviewed validation studies, neither of which bioresonance companies have produced. The "German technology" claim creates a false association with engineering rigor; Germany's own medical establishment rejects bioresonance.

## Vitals operates a supplement sales funnel, not a diagnostic service

**Vitals Brand (Thailand) Company Limited** is a small Bangkok-based operation with approximately **8 employees**, founded by Charlie Temple, whose background is in fitness businesses (Flex Studio, Platinum Fitness, PURE Group)—not medical diagnostics, nutrition science, or laboratory testing. The company's CMO, Radek Krasny, was caught **leaving a 5-star review on their own Google Play app**, a significant integrity red flag.

The company's own website explicitly describes their model: "We combine wellness testing, expert-led recommendations, and a carefully curated selection of best-in-market supplement brands with our own proprietary Vitals blends." This is the test-to-supplement pipeline laid bare: administer a pseudoscientific test → generate results inevitably showing "deficiencies" → recommend supplements from their own shop → provide discount codes to drive purchases → recommend retesting every few months.

This business model is replicated globally by companies including Check My Body Health (UK), 5Strands (USA), and Test Your Intolerance. The pattern is well-documented. As one critical analysis noted: "At best, results are randomized guesses dressed up with colourful charts meant to dazzle you into buying supplements. The 'imbalances' flagged almost always lead to supplement recommendations. This is classic upselling: create a problem, then sell the fix."

The Better Business Bureau received **23 complaints** about Modern Allergy Management, a similar bioresonance hair testing company, including one case where a six-year-old child's test recommended a "male vigor sex-enhancement supplement"—illustrating the random, algorithmically-generated nature of these reports. No independent reviews, media coverage, or third-party assessments of Vitals Thailand were found despite their positioning as "the region's leading expert platform."

## What this means for BodyCypher's strategy

Blood-based testing and bioresonance hair testing are not on the same scientific spectrum. They are not alternatives, complements, or competitors in any meaningful sense. Blood testing measures real analytes with validated instruments, standardized reference ranges, and reproducible results in CLIA-certified laboratories. Bioresonance measures nothing.

The strategic implication is threefold. First, **bioresonance is not a legitimate alternative** to blood testing—it cannot generate accurate supplement recommendations because its diagnostic output is indistinguishable from random noise. Second, the existence of companies like Vitals represents a **market education opportunity** for BodyCypher: consumers who have experienced bioresonance testing and been disappointed by vague or contradictory results are primed for a science-backed alternative. Third, **the supplement sales funnel model** used by Vitals is worth studying—not to replicate the pseudoscientific testing, but to understand how the test-to-recommendation-to-purchase pipeline works commercially while building it on legitimate diagnostic foundations.

The competitive moat for BodyCypher is scientific validity itself. In a market where consumers are increasingly skeptical of wellness pseudoscience, positioning explicitly against bioresonance—with transparent methodology, validated biomarkers, and reproducible results—is both ethically sound and commercially differentiated.
