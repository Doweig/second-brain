# Product Review: Kiro Bio

*Date: February 19, 2026 | Plan: Free (patient-facing tier) | Data submitted: N Health PDF*

---

## Company Context

Kiro Bio (formerly Byond) is a Paris-based B2B digital medicine company founded in 2019. Raised ~EUR 17M total, including a EUR 13.8M Series A in March 2023 led by Sofinnova Partners (their first Digital Medicine Strategy investment). 25 employees. Claims "millions of users" through lab partnerships (Labio, Unilabs, American Hospital of Paris). Revenue model: hospitals and labs subscribe on a fee-per-report basis. Patient-facing product is free. B2B is the business; B2C is the distribution layer.

---

## Onboarding

**How did I submit data?** PDF upload of N Health results.
**What personal info did they ask?** Age, sex, name. That's it. Very minimal intake, no health goals, no medications, no supplements, no lifestyle context.
**How long from signup to first result?** Near-instant. Upload creates a new report immediately.
**Did it recognize my lab format?** Appeared to parse the N Health PDF successfully. Each upload creates a separate report.
**First impression:** Simple, fast, no friction. Clearly optimized for the B2B use case where a lab sends results to patients through Kiro's interface. Not designed for a consumer who wants to understand themselves.

---

## Dashboard / global view

**What's the main screen?** A scrollable list of biomarkers showing in-range vs. out-of-range status. No overall health score, no organ system summary, no executive overview.
**Global score?** None.
**Category or organ scores?** No scores. There is some grouping, but it follows clinical laboratory categories (hematology, biochemistry, renal function, calcium, protein) rather than user-friendly organ-based or goal-based organization. The grouping feels like it mirrors how a lab technician would sort results, not how a patient thinks about their health.
**Visual system?** Color-coded in-range/out-of-range indicators. You can scroll quickly and see at a glance what's flagged. Dense, compact layout.
**What stands out? What's missing?** The density is the feature. It's designed for quick scanning, which is useful for a doctor reviewing results with a patient. What's completely absent: any synthesis, any "so what," any prioritization. It's a prettier version of the PDF, not an interpretation of it.

---

## Biomarker detail

**How many biomarkers recognized?** Appears to handle standard lab panels. Exact count not clear from this session.
**Reference ranges:** Shows ranges but does not specify whether they are standard lab ranges or optimal/functional ranges. No labeling to distinguish.
**Visualization:** Dense view only. No alternative layouts, no zoomed-in marker views with context. What you see is what you get.
**Explanation quality:** For out-of-range markers, there is a "detail" button that provides a brief explanation. Clicking "more" redirects to a government health website. The explanations are generic, not personalized to the user's values or context.
**What's good about this view?** Speed. A doctor (Dr. Chamapa reviewed it) said the interface was easy to read. The at-a-glance in/out-of-range scan works well for clinical workflows.
**What's bad or missing?** No optimal ranges. No personalization. No context about what the marker means for *you*. The external redirect for deeper info feels like an incomplete product -- like they built the data display layer but stopped before the intelligence layer.

---

## Action plan / recommendations

**Is there a synthesized action plan?** No.
**How is it organized?** N/A.
**Is it cross-referenced across biomarkers?** N/A.
**Do they prioritize?** N/A.

**Specificity (1-5):** 1/5
*No action plan exists. Zero recommendations. The product stops at "here are your numbers."*

**Personalization (1-5):** 1/5
*Minimal intake (age, sex, name) means there's almost nothing to personalize against. The output would be the same for any two people of the same age and sex.*

**What do they recommend?** Nothing. No supplements, no dietary guidance, no lifestyle changes, no follow-up testing suggestions. This is purely a data display product.

---

## Tracking and history

**Longitudinal trends?** The Google Play listing and marketing claim historical tracking and trend comparison. Each PDF upload creates a separate report, so in theory you could build a history. Not tested in this session.
**Can I upload older results?** Yes, each upload creates a new report.
**Trend visualization quality:** Unknown from this session. Marketing suggests it exists but unclear how well it works.

---

## AI / interactive features

**AI chat or Q&A?** None visible in the free patient-facing tier. Kiro's marketing mentions an AI assistant and "clinical decision support tools," but these appear to be B2B features available to labs and physicians, not exposed to patients.
**Other notable features?** The company has 7 patents and claims medically-validated algorithms that increase true positive detection rates by 25%. This is the B2B value prop: helping labs catch things they'd otherwise miss. None of this surfaces in the patient experience.

---

## Supplement sales

**Do they sell supplements?** No.
**Upsell aggressiveness (1-5):** 1/5
*No selling of any kind. The product is free for patients. Revenue comes from labs paying per report.*

---

## Overall

**Design quality (1-5):** 3/5
*Clean, functional, but utilitarian. The dense view works for its purpose (doctor reviewing with patient) but doesn't feel consumer-grade. No delight factor.*

**Mobile experience:** Web-based. Has Android app (on Google Play). Not tested natively.

**Best thing about this product:**
Speed and clarity of the in-range/out-of-range scan. A doctor can pull this up, scroll through quickly, and see immediately what needs attention. The B2B model is smart: labs get a better patient interface without building one, patients get free access, Kiro gets distribution. The American Hospital of Paris partnership validates clinical credibility.

**Biggest gap or disappointment:**
This is a data display product, not an interpretation product. It solves "your PDF is hard to read" but doesn't touch "what should I do about it." For [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]]'s purposes, Kiro validates that the market wants better result visualization, but it also proves that visualization alone isn't enough. There's no action plan, no AI interpretation, no supplement guidance, no longitudinal intelligence. It's a nicer container for the same data.

**What should BodyCypher steal?**
The at-a-glance in/out-of-range visual scan. Dr. Chamapa's positive reaction to the interface is a signal. The ability to quickly see "these 5 things are flagged" before diving into detail is useful. The onboarding simplicity (upload PDF, get results instantly) is also worth noting, though BodyCypher needs much richer intake data.

**What should BodyCypher NOT copy?**
The "display-only" approach. Kiro stops exactly where BodyCypher's value proposition begins. Also: the crude clinical grouping (hematology, biochemistry) rather than user-meaningful categories (energy, hormones, heart health). And the external redirect for explanations, which feels like the product ran out of budget.

**Overall rating (1-10):** 3/10
*As a B2B lab visualization tool, it's probably a 6. As a consumer health product competing for the attention of optimizers, it's a 3. It does one thing (display results clearly) and does it adequately, but offers nothing beyond that. For BodyCypher benchmarking purposes, this sits at the very bottom of the interpretation spectrum, well below even InsideTracker's basic per-marker recommendations.*

---

## BodyCypher Competitive Positioning Note

Kiro occupies the "Layer 1" of the value stack: data display. BodyCypher's product starts at Layer 2 (interpretation) and aims for Layer 3 (actionable protocols). The gap between Kiro and BodyCypher should be enormous by design. Kiro is not a competitor; it's a reminder of how low the bar currently is, especially in non-US markets. The fact that this product raised EUR 17M and partnered with the American Hospital of Paris while offering zero interpretation to patients shows how much the B2B lab market values even modest UX improvements, and how completely the patient intelligence layer remains unsolved.
