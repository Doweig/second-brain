# Product Review: InsideTracker

*Date: February 2026 | Plan: Paid — $150 membership (blood test + membership bundle ~$500–600) | Data submitted: 2 blood test PDFs (Path Lab, N Health), ~28 biomarkers recognized*

---

## Onboarding

**How did I submit data?** PDF upload. Tried [[bdms-wellness|BDMS]] report first — very buggy, couldn't recognize many pages including testosterone (a major biomarker). Cancelled that one. Used Path Lab and N Health reports instead, which parsed better and extracted testosterone successfully.

**What personal info did they ask?** A lot. Ethnicity (interesting touch), date of birth, sex, body weight, height, resting heart rate, body fat percentage, diet, restaurant frequency, exercise frequency and type, sleep duration, tobacco use, daily sun exposure. Also asked about supplements — which ones I take (B complex, calcium, creatine, fish oil, iron, magnesium, multivitamin, probiotics, protein, B12, vitamin C, vitamin D) and dosage ranges (e.g., magnesium: <100mg, 100–400mg, >400mg). Goals were collected too. They also prompted for DNA upload (23andMe / Ancestry.com only — my Circle DNA wasn't recognized).

**Food survey (notable):** First time seeing this level of detail. Slider with 4 positions per food category. Categories: fruits, vegetables, nuts, grains, beans, soy, dairy, eggs, fish, poultry, shellfish, pork. Drinks: soda, sports drinks, caffeine, alcohol, sweets.

**Food survey critique:** The slider units are inconsistent — e.g., "less than 4 times per week" then "1 time per day" then "2 times per day." Mixing weekly and daily units is confusing. Would be better to keep everything in the same unit with more gradient options. Ended up clicking somewhat randomly because conversions were too annoying. Needs more intuitive phrasing like "less than 1 per day / 1 per day / 2 per day."

**How long from signup to first result?** Relatively quick once PDFs were accepted.

**Did it recognize my lab format?** Mixed. BDMS report was very buggy — many pages unrecognized, including testosterone. Path Lab and N Health worked better. Still not flawless.

**First impression:** Thorough onboarding that collects a lot of context. The food survey is ambitious but poorly executed with unit inconsistency. DNA upload too limited (only 23andMe/Ancestry).

---

## Dashboard / global view

**What's the main screen?** The dashboard is disappointing — it's more of a marketing page than a health dashboard. Prominent elements: "Refer a friend" (20% off), "Download mobile app," achievements badges, and article/video content. Very little about *me*.

**Global score?** "Inner Age" is mentioned but not prominently displayed. No clear overall health score.

**Category or organ scores?** Categories visible when clicking into results: Heart Health, Metabolism, Gut Health, Inflammation, Fitness, Cognition, Endurance, Hormone Balance, Recovery, Sleep.

**Biomarker summary:** At Risk: 1 | Needs Improvement: 6 | Optimized: 21. But the "1 at risk" was confusing — it showed as 2 red items in the list, but it's the same biomarker (LDL cholesterol at 130) appearing in two categories.

**Visual system:** Traffic light — red (at risk), yellow (needs improvement), green (optimized).

**Three view modes:**
- **Table view:** Very condensed, dense, shows every test and data point. Best for thorough review.
- **Category view:** Aggregated per organ/system. Good for non-doctors who want a higher-level picture.
- **List view:** Card per biomarker with small charts. Weird middle ground — endless scrolling, not particularly useful. Neither as dense as table nor as synthesized as category.

**What stands out?** The actual results page (not dashboard) is where the real content lives. Dashboard feels like it's about InsideTracker, not about me.

**What's missing?** A proper dashboard with my health front and center. Current dashboard prioritizes referrals and product promotion.

## Biomarker detail

**How many biomarkers recognized?** ~28 (21 optimized + 6 needs improvement + 1 at risk).

**Reference ranges:** Standard lab ranges PLUS optimal/functional ranges. Notably stricter than other products — e.g., LDL at 130 was flagged as "very high, you should get this lower" when other apps said it was borderline or fine. This is actually a positive: given my active lifestyle and health goals, having tighter optimal ranges makes sense.

**Testosterone example (builds trust):** Optimal range listed as 500–1000 ng/dL, which is actually a good, informed range. Many products consider anything above the clinical low as "fine," but InsideTracker acknowledges you don't want to be below 500 even if you're not clinically deficient. This made me trust the system more.

**Visualization:** Multiple elements per biomarker:
- History graph with zones (optimized / normal / borderline / high-low)
- **Population distribution chart (standout feature):** X-axis = biomarker value, Y-axis = % of population in that bucket. Filtered by sex and age (±1 year, e.g., male age 34–36). Shows where I fall relative to InsideTracker's customer base (likely a health-conscious population). For LDL, I was slightly above the median — reassuring given the population skews fit.

**Explanation quality:** Decent. Scientific sources are presented well — each source has a snippet/paragraph explaining what it says, not just a raw citation link. The "Learn" tab itself is weak (mostly internal article links, probably for SEO), but the "Results" tab of each biomarker has better educational content than the dedicated Learn tab.

**What's good about this view?** Population distribution comparison is excellent. Strict optimal ranges build trust. Source presentation with snippets is well done.

**What's bad or missing?** LDL appearing as 2 red items across categories is confusing. The Learn tab is nearly useless compared to the Results tab content.

## Action plan / recommendations

**Is there a synthesized action plan?** No global action plan. Recommendations are per-biomarker only. No consolidated "here's what you should do" view across all your results. This is a significant gap.

**How is it organized?** Per biomarker, split into 4 categories: Exercise, Nutrition (food), Supplements, Lifestyle. There's also an "All" view that combines and ranks everything by impact.

**Is it cross-referenced across biomarkers?** Not clearly. Each biomarker has its own recommendations.

**Do they prioritize?** Yes — within each biomarker, recommendations are ranked by impact score (e.g., "olive oil: 9.9 impact" vs. "drink oolong tea: 7 impact"). The "All" combined view ranks across categories, showing that for LDL, sleep was #1 impact and olive oil was #2.

**Specificity (1-5):** 3.5/5
*They recommend specific foods and supplements with impact rankings, but dosages aren't always clear. Exercise recommendations are generic (e.g., "high-intensity workout").*

**Personalization (1-5):** 3/5
*Recommendations feel somewhat tailored to biomarker values but it's unclear how much the extensive onboarding data (food survey, goals, lifestyle) actually influences recommendations.*

**What do they recommend?** Specific foods (olive oil, oolong tea, etc.) with impact scores, supplements ranked by impact, exercise types, sleep optimization. The impact ranking system is genuinely useful.

**Nutrition tab (weak):** Lets you filter food recommendations by biomarker or goal — but each filter dumps ~20 foods. Across multiple biomarkers and goals, it becomes an unmanageable list. Nobody is going to memorize or follow 60+ food recommendations. This feature is effectively useless.

## Tracking and history

**Longitudinal trends?** Yes — graphs per biomarker showing history with zone overlays (optimized/normal/borderline/high-low).

**Can I upload older results?** Yes, multiple PDFs accepted (though parsing quality varies by lab).

**Trend visualization quality:** Looks decent. Graphs are functional with clear zone delineation.

---

## AI / interactive features

**AI chat or Q&A?** Yes — "Ask Tara" (their AI assistant). Can ask questions about your blood results. Quality seems basic — at this point, having an AI with your blood results is table stakes.

**Missing transparency:** No information about which AI model or infrastructure powers Tara. For power users especially, knowing whether it's GPT, Gemini, Claude, or a fine-tuned model matters. At minimum, disclosing this would build trust.

**Other notable features?** PDF download of results (just a table view — not very useful). No global action plan export.

---

## Supplement sales

**Do they sell supplements?** Not prominently visible — the platform is very US-centric so many products/services may not be accessible from Thailand.

**Upsell aggressiveness (1-5):** 3/5
*They don't push supplements hard, but they heavily upsell additional blood tests. Test upsells appear at the bottom of individual biomarker pages and throughout the experience.*

---

## Overall

**Design quality (1-5):** 3/5
*It's busy, but compared to competitors it's relatively decent. The population distribution chart is well-designed. Dashboard is the weakest part visually.*

**Mobile experience:** Mobile app exists (prompted to download on dashboard). Not tested in depth.

**Best thing about this product:** The population distribution chart comparing you to people of your age/sex is excellent and builds real context. The impact-ranked recommendations per biomarker are genuinely useful. Strict optimal ranges (especially testosterone) inspire trust.

**Biggest gap or disappointment:** No global, synthesized action plan. The dashboard is a marketing page, not a health dashboard. The nutrition tab with 20+ foods per filter is impractical. List view is a useless middle ground. PDF parsing is unreliable for non-US labs.

**What should [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]] steal?**
- Population distribution chart (age/sex filtered) for biomarker context
- Impact-ranked recommendations (the "All" view combining exercise/nutrition/supplements/lifestyle sorted by impact)
- Strict optimal ranges that account for lifestyle/goals, not just clinical thresholds
- Source snippets alongside citations (not just links)
- The concept of collecting detailed onboarding data (but execute the food survey better)

**What should BodyCypher NOT copy?**
- Dashboard as marketing page — put the user's health front and center
- The nutrition tab dumping 20+ foods per filter with no prioritization
- List view (pick table for density or category for synthesis, skip the middle)
- US-centric supplement/test upsell infrastructure
- Inconsistent units in food surveys
- Limited DNA upload compatibility

**Overall rating (1-10):** 6.5/10
