# BodyCypher Thailand: regulatory and legal roadmap for a health tech dual-entity startup

**A foreign-founded health tech company interpreting blood tests and recommending supplements in Thailand faces a dense but navigable regulatory landscape across seven major domains.** The dual-entity model — a BOI-promoted tech company paired with a separately licensed Thai medical entity — is the most viable structure for maintaining foreign ownership while complying with healthcare regulations. With **3–5 million THB** initial capital, BOI promotion under Activity 8.1.1 is achievable, but the founder must carefully manage classification boundaries between wellness technology and medical practice, as missteps carry criminal penalties including imprisonment. This report provides a comprehensive, section-by-section analysis of every major regulatory question.

---

## 1. BOI promotion is accessible at 3–5 million THB for a digital health platform

The Board of Investment restructured its categories in 2024 under Announcement No. Sor 2/2567. The old categories (5.7, 5.8, 5.9) were consolidated, and [[bodycypher|BodyCypher]]'s tech entity should target **Activity 8.1.1: Development of Software or Platforms for Digital Services**, classified as an **A2 incentive tier**. This covers computer programs, applications, and platforms facilitating exchange of information or services through electronic networks — squarely fitting an app-based blood test interpretation platform.

**Capital requirements are surprisingly low for digital businesses.** Unlike manufacturing categories requiring heavy equipment investment, Activity 8.1.1 measures "investment" primarily through **Thai IT personnel salaries: minimum THB 1.5 million per year** in salaries for Thai IT staff hired after the BOI application. The CIT exemption cap is calculated at 100% of these salaries plus 200% of training expenses. There is no traditional minimum capital expenditure requirement beyond the standard **THB 1 million** registered capital floor. A founder with 3–5 million THB can comfortably cover initial registered capital, Thai developer salaries, and operational setup. Multiple Thai law firms confirm that tech companies regularly obtain BOI promotion at this investment level.

**BOI grants 100% foreign ownership** for Activity 8.1.1, completely exempting the promoted company from Foreign Business Act restrictions. The exemption applies specifically to the promoted activity — meaning supplement retail operations would NOT be covered and must be structured separately. Additional BOI benefits include:

- **8-year corporate income tax exemption** (capped based on Thai IT salary expenditure)
- **Non-Immigrant IB Visa** for the founder, renewable annually
- **No 4:1 Thai-to-foreign worker ratio** requirement (ratio negotiated based on project needs)
- **No THB 2 million registered capital per foreign worker** requirement (standard rule waived)
- **Fast-track work permits** through the BOI One-Stop Service Center, often processed in a single day
- Minimum salary for BOI foreign executives: **THB 150,000/month**; specialists: **THB 50,000/month**

The **SMART Visa S (Startup)** is an alternative pathway offering a 2-year renewable visa without a separate work permit, requiring ≥25% share ownership or directorship, THB 600,000 in savings, and certification from NIA or DEPA. This may be worth pursuing alongside or instead of the standard BOI visa.

**Timeline for BOI approval runs 2–3 months** for digital services companies: 2–4 weeks for application preparation, 45–90 days for BOI review, and 1–2 weeks for certificate issuance. The company can then incorporate, inject foreign capital (with proof via bank credit advice), and obtain a Foreign Business Certificate from the DBD within 30 days.

**Critical exclusion**: BOI promotion under 8.1.1 does NOT cover retail/wholesale sales, including online supplement sales. Any supplement revenue stream must be structurally separated from the promoted tech activities. If BodyCypher's primary revenue derives from supplement commissions rather than software/platform fees, the tech entity's BOI eligibility could be challenged.

Both entities can theoretically receive separate BOI promotions — the tech entity under 8.1.1 and the medical entity under a medical services category in Section 7 — though the medical entity's eligibility requires separate assessment and typically demands a licensed medical facility with higher investment thresholds.

## 2. The dual-entity model is pragmatically sound but demands careful structuring

The dual-entity structure — a foreign-owned BOI-promoted tech company plus a Thai-majority medical entity — is a **commonly used and legally recognized approach** for foreign health tech founders in Thailand. It is not a nominee arrangement; it is a legitimate separation of distinct regulatory activities.

**Why this structure works best:** The Foreign Business Act restricts foreign ownership to 49% in most service businesses, including healthcare. Software development qualifies for BOI promotion enabling 100% foreign ownership. This creates a natural split: the tech entity owns the app, brand, AI algorithms, and IP, while the medical entity holds healthcare licenses and employs medical professionals. The structure provides **risk segregation** between tech operations and medical liability, **tax optimization** through BOI incentives on the tech entity, and **regulatory compliance** across both the technology and healthcare frameworks.

**The partnership agreement between entities** should take the form of a technology licensing agreement (tech entity licenses platform access to the medical entity) and a service-level agreement (tech entity provides platform maintenance, data processing, and technical support). Revenue flows typically work through licensing fees or service fees from the medical entity to the tech entity. Every fee must be set at **arm's length** per transfer pricing requirements, and the pricing methodology must be documented using one of five OECD-recognized methods (CUP, Resale Price, Cost Plus, TNMM, or Profit Split).

**Transfer pricing scrutiny is real.** Section 71 bis of the Revenue Code (enacted 2018, effective 2019) empowers the Thai Revenue Department to adjust income and expenses when related-party transactions deviate from arm's length pricing. "Related party" includes entities where one holds >50% shares in another, entities with a common >50% shareholder, or entities related by management/control. Even if formal shareholding thresholds are not met, if the foreign founder effectively controls both entities, they will likely be deemed related parties. Companies with revenue ≥ **THB 200 million** must file a Transfer Pricing Disclosure Form; those below this threshold are exempt from mandatory disclosure but must still price at arm's length. Penalties for non-compliance include **THB 200,000 per instance** for filing failures, **up to 200% penalty** on additional tax assessed, and **1.5% monthly surcharge** on underpaid tax.

**Alternative structures and why they fall short:**

- **Single entity with both licenses**: Requires Thai-majority ownership (51%+), causing the founder to lose control of IP and technology
- **Branch office**: Exposes the foreign parent to full Thai liability; healthcare licensing typically requires Thai incorporation
- **Joint venture**: Less stable for ongoing platform business; still subject to FBA if foreign-majority
- **VIE (Variable Interest Entity)**: **High risk** — could be challenged as a de facto nominee arrangement under FBA Sections 36–37, carrying criminal penalties including imprisonment up to 3 years and fines up to THB 1 million. Thailand's crackdown on nominees is intensifying, with proposed AMLO amendments that would enable asset seizure

## 3. A licensed doctor is not required as company director — but IS required as medical facility operator

This distinction is critical and often confused. Thai law separates two entirely different roles with different legal requirements.

**Company board directors** (กรรมการบริษัท) are governed by the Civil and Commercial Code and the company's Articles of Association. **No medical qualification is required.** A company director manages corporate affairs — finances, contracts, operations — and can be a foreign national. There is no nationality requirement for directors of Thai private limited companies.

**The medical facility operator** (ผู้ดำเนินการสถานพยาบาล), by contrast, is a regulatory role mandated by the **Medical Facility Act B.E. 2541 (1998)**. Any entity operating as a "medical establishment" (สถานพยาบาล) needs two separate licenses: a facility operating license (Form SorPor. 7, valid 10 years, held by the entity) and a medical director license (Form SorPor. 19, valid 2 years). The medical director **must hold a valid license to practice medicine** from the Medical Council of Thailand, must be physically present during operating hours, and must actively supervise all medical treatments. "License lending" — appointing a ghost medical director — is illegal, punishable by up to **2 years imprisonment and THB 40,000 fine** for both the owner and the absent doctor.

**The pivotal question is whether BodyCypher's app triggers classification as a "medical establishment."** This depends on what the app actually does:

Factors **triggering** medical establishment classification include providing diagnosis of disease, treatment recommendations, or direct patient-doctor consultations (telemedicine). Factors **avoiding** this classification include providing general wellness information, using disclaimers, presenting blood test interpretations as educational content rather than medical diagnosis, and recommending dietary supplements (not prescription drugs).

The Thai FDA has issued **Guidelines for Determining Health-Type Software Products** (updated 2024). Software performing medical diagnostics (e.g., ECG analysis, diabetic retinopathy screening) is classified as a **medical device** under the Medical Device Act B.E. 2551. Software for general wellness monitoring is classified as non-medical. An app interpreting blood test results sits in a **grey area**: if it provides diagnostic conclusions ("you have iron deficiency anemia"), it likely qualifies as a medical device; if it displays results with general educational information ("your iron levels are below the general reference range"), it may not.

**If [[decypher|the app]] merely connects users to licensed medical facilities/doctors** — acting as a digital platform rather than a medical provider — it falls under the **Royal Decree on Digital Platforms** regulated by ETDA, not the Medical Facility Act. Per Tilleke & Gibbins analysis: "As actual medical services are not offered or provided, these digital platforms do not have to be regulated under the Medical Facility Act of Thailand."

**Recommended approach**: Structure the tech entity's app as a wellness/information platform. Route any individualized medical consultations or doctor reviews through the medical entity's licensed facility as a separate service. Submit a Product Classification Application to the Thai FDA to obtain definitive classification of the software.

## 4. PDPA compliance demands a DPO and granular consent for blood test data

BodyCypher **almost certainly must appoint a Data Protection Officer**. Section 41(3) of the PDPA mandates DPO appointment when the core activity of the data controller is the collection, use, or disclosure of sensitive personal data under Section 26. Blood test results are unambiguously "health data" — explicitly listed as sensitive personal data. Since BodyCypher's entire business revolves around processing health data, this trigger is met regardless of data volume. The penalty for failure to appoint a DPO is an administrative fine of up to **THB 1,000,000**. Thailand's first major PDPA enforcement case (August 2024, **THB 7 million** total fine) specifically cited failure to appoint a DPO among the violations.

**The DPO does not need to be a doctor.** The PDPA specifies DPO duties but not formal qualifications — no medical, legal, or technical certification is required by statute. Recommended qualifications include knowledge of PDPA and applicable laws, understanding of IT and data security, and communication skills with executives and regulators. The DPO **can be an external consultant or outsourced provider** (explicitly permitted by Section 41), and multiple affiliated companies may share a single DPO. A foreign national can serve as DPO, though practical considerations (Thai language ability, familiarity with Thai regulatory culture) matter. The PDPC may later issue stricter qualification regulations, so early appointment could provide grandfathering benefits.

**Health data processing requires explicit consent** under Section 26, which prohibits collection of sensitive personal data without it. "Explicit consent" means an express statement — a clear affirmative action such as clicking an unchecked checkbox. Pre-ticked boxes are not valid. Consent must be clearly separated from other matters (cannot be bundled with terms and conditions), use clear and plain language, and be freely given — it cannot be made conditional on service provision. Each distinct processing purpose requires separate consent. Using blood test data to train AI models, for instance, requires **additional explicit consent specifically for that purpose**, separate from the consent to process data for supplement recommendations.

**Privacy notices** (Section 23) must disclose: purpose of collection, categories of data collected, retention period, categories of recipients, DPO contact information, and data subject rights — all provided prior to or at the time of collection.

**Cross-border data transfers** present a nuanced challenge. The PDPC has **not yet published a whitelist** of countries with adequate data protection standards as of February 2026. However, a critical December 2023 notification provides a practical carve-out: data stored on overseas cloud servers (AWS, Google Cloud) where **no third party has access** — only the data controller's personnel — is excluded from the definition of "cross-border transfer." This means standard cloud hosting may not trigger transfer rules. However, **sending data to AI APIs** (e.g., OpenAI) for processing almost certainly constitutes a cross-border transfer, since data is actively processed by a third party abroad. Until the whitelist is published, BodyCypher should use **Standard Contractual Clauses** (based on EU or ASEAN models) with overseas processors, and obtain explicit consent disclosing the cross-border transfer and lack of adequacy determination.

**PDPA penalties have teeth.** Administrative fines reach up to **THB 5 million per violation**. Criminal penalties for misuse of sensitive personal data include imprisonment up to **1 year** and fines up to THB 1 million. Civil liability includes actual damages plus punitive damages of up to **2× actual compensation**. The PDPC issued **THB 21.5 million** in total fines across 5 cases in 2025 alone, and healthcare is identified as a priority enforcement sector. Data breach notification must occur **within 72 hours** of becoming aware of a breach, with notification to affected data subjects required if the breach poses high risk to their rights.

## 5. Liability cannot be disclaimed for death or bodily injury — and the burden of proof falls on the company

This is perhaps the most consequential legal reality for BodyCypher. **Section 8 of the Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 absolutely prohibits** any disclaimer, announcement, or notice that excludes or restricts liability for loss of life, body, or health resulting from deliberate or negligent action. This prohibition is categorical — no contractual language, however carefully drafted, can override it. Section 22 of the Product Liability Act B.E. 2551 separately renders void any agreement to limit liability for damages from product use.

If a user suffers adverse health effects or dies from following the app's supplement recommendations, **multiple parties face concurrent liability**:

The **tech company** faces tort liability under CCC Section 420 (negligent injury) and potentially strict liability under the Product Liability Act (if the app/AI is classified as a defective "product") or under CCC Section 437 (possessor of dangerous property). Whether an AI algorithm constitutes a "product" under the Product Liability Act remains an open legal question in Thailand — tangible AI products clearly fall within scope, but algorithms and source code arguably do not. Thailand's **Draft Artificial Intelligence Bill (2025)** would resolve this by mandating that all AI-generated actions be attributable to a human and imposing a "duty of care" framework.

The **medical entity** faces liability as a healthcare service provider. A landmark **2022 Supreme Court ruling** confirmed that private healthcare providers are "service providers" under the Consumer Case Procedure Act, triggering a critical procedural consequence: the **burden of proof shifts to the healthcare provider** to prove they were NOT negligent. Patient injury creates a presumption of negligence. The medical entity also bears **vicarious liability under CCC Section 425** for wrongful acts of employed doctors.

The **reviewing doctor** faces personal tort liability, potential criminal prosecution under Penal Code Sections 291 (negligent death) and 300 (negligent bodily injury), and disciplinary action from the Medical Council. The doctor bears responsibility for the final recommendation regardless of whether AI generated the initial suggestion.

**Company directors** face potential personal liability under CCC Sections 1167–1168 when acting beyond authority or breaching fiduciary duties. The 2017 reform abolished strict liability presumptions for directors — prosecutors must now prove the offense resulted from the director's direct instructions, actions, or failures to act. However, in severe consumer protection cases involving fatalities, directors may still face criminal liability and punitive damages.

**The dual-entity structure may not shield the tech company from the medical entity's liability.** Under CCC Sections 425 and 427, if the tech company exerts significant control over the medical entity or was "at fault in regard to the work ordered or to instructions or to the selection of the contractor," vicarious liability attaches. Under Section 432, where several persons are jointly bound for a wrongful act, they are **jointly and severally liable**.

**What CAN be done to minimize exposure:**

- **Informed consent disclosures** (not liability waivers) explaining AI limitations, risks of supplement use, and importance of consulting additional medical professionals
- **Clear scope-of-service definitions** framing the service as wellness/informational — though courts examine substance over form
- **Arbitration clauses** are enforceable under the Arbitration Act B.E. 2545, but consumer arbitration clauses face heightened scrutiny and may be struck down if they impose undue burdens
- **Limitation of damages** for non-bodily-harm claims (economic/consequential losses) may be enforceable if "fair and reasonable"
- **Comprehensive insurance portfolio**: medical malpractice insurance, professional indemnity (E&O), product liability insurance, D&O insurance, and cyber insurance — none are legally required but all are strongly recommended

**There are no Thai court precedents directly addressing health tech or AI liability.** The 2022 Supreme Court medical malpractice ruling is the closest analogue, and the Product Liability Act has generated few landmark decisions as most cases settle. This legal vacuum creates both risk (unpredictable outcomes) and opportunity (ability to set favorable precedent through careful structuring).

## 6. Thai FDA treats supplement recommendations as advertising — and blood test interpretation may constitute practicing medicine

Even if BodyCypher only **recommends** supplements without selling them, regulatory risk is substantial. Under **Section 41 of the Food Act B.E. 2522**, advertising is defined to include any communication "by other means for business purposes" about the quality, usefulness, or indication of food. The Thai FDA evaluates claims based on **consumer perception, not manufacturer intention** (per MOPH Notification No. 447 B.E. 2566). If the app recommends specific supplement products or brands based on blood test results for commercial purposes — including through affiliate commissions — this almost certainly constitutes advertising requiring **prior FDA approval**. Each recommendation would need an FDA advertising approval number (Khor.).

**Health claims are tightly restricted** under the current framework (Notification 447). Three categories exist: nutrient function claims (generally allowed, e.g., "Vitamin A contributes to normal vision"), other function claims (restricted, require scientific substantiation and pre-approval), and reduction of disease risk claims (near-prohibition for food supplements). Claims implying **prevention, treatment, cure, or mitigation of disease are strictly prohibited** for food supplements. The app cannot say "take this supplement to treat your iron deficiency" — only carefully worded, pre-approved nutrient function claims are relatively safe. Violations carry imprisonment up to **3 years and/or fines up to THB 30,000** for false or deceptive advertising.

**The more dangerous risk is unauthorized practice of medicine.** The Medical Profession Act defines medical practice to include examination, diagnosis, treatment, and health promotion performed on humans. If the app interprets blood test results and recommends specific supplements to address identified deficiencies, this could constitute diagnosis plus treatment recommendation — **practicing medicine without a license**, carrying penalties of up to **3 years imprisonment and/or THB 30,000 fine**. The 2021 Telemedicine Notification further requires that healthcare applications partner with licensed medical facilities; standalone apps cannot provide telemedicine services.

**If BodyCypher sells or ships supplements**, the requirements multiply dramatically: FDA product registration for each supplement (obtaining a Food Serial Number), import license (Form Orr. 7, valid 3 years), warehouse license, GMP/HACCP certification from internationally recognized bodies, Thai-language labeling meeting detailed requirements under Notification 293, and separate advertising licenses for each product. Registration takes 1–4 months for simple products and 6–12 months for complex ones, costing approximately **THB 30,000–100,000** per product.

**An affiliate model** (recommending and linking to third-party sellers) still likely constitutes advertising under the Food Act's broad definition and may trigger obligations under the Direct Sales and Direct Marketing Act B.E. 2545 (registration required if annual income >THB 1.8 million) and the Royal Decree on Digital Platform Services (notification to ETDA, annual reporting, and mandatory terms).

The **safest approach** for the tech entity: frame recommendations as general educational wellness information ("foods and supplements commonly associated with supporting iron levels"), avoid naming specific products or brands without FDA advertising approval, route any personalized supplement recommendations through the licensed medical entity's doctors, and clearly disclaim that information is educational rather than medical advice — while acknowledging that courts will examine substance over form.

## 7. The Foreign Business Act is a barrier without BOI — and nominee arrangements carry severe criminal penalties

BodyCypher's tech company **falls under List 3, Item 21 ("other service businesses")** of the Foreign Business Act B.E. 2542. The Ministry of Commerce interprets "service businesses" very broadly, capturing software services, internet services, digital platforms, and SaaS operations. While a 2019 Ministerial Regulation exempted certain narrow software categories (data analytics, cybersecurity, industrial manufacturing support), a health tech app would **likely NOT qualify** for these exemptions.

**BOI promotion provides a complete exemption.** Under Section 12 of the FBA, a BOI-promoted company can obtain a Foreign Business Certificate (not a license — the distinction matters) from the DBD within 30 days, enabling 100% foreign ownership throughout the promotion period. This exemption applies only to the specific promoted activity; non-promoted activities remain subject to FBA restrictions. Without BOI, the alternative is a Foreign Business License requiring minimum registered capital of **THB 3 million per restricted activity** and approval from the Foreign Business Committee (2–4 month process).

**Nominee arrangements are illegal and enforcement is at historic highs.** Statutory penalties under Section 36 include imprisonment up to **3 years**, fines of THB 100,000 to **THB 1 million**, and court-ordered dissolution of the company — applying to both the Thai nominee and the foreign investor. In September 2024, a landmark case resulted in **23 defendants sentenced to 10 years imprisonment** (reduced to 5 years suspended), each fined THB 200,000, with all companies ordered dissolved. In April 2025, the Ministry of Commerce announced inspections of **46,918 business entities**. Proposed AMLO amendments would make FBA violations a predicate offense under the Anti-Money Laundering Act, enabling asset seizure. Authorities detect nominees through financial capacity analysis, origin-of-funds investigations, review of contracts (blank share transfers, sham loan agreements), and dividend distribution analysis.

**The US-Thailand Treaty of Amity** allows US citizens and US-majority-owned companies to hold 100% ownership in most sectors without BOI, but this is only available to US nationals. For other foreign nationals, BOI promotion remains the most reliable pathway to foreign ownership and FBA exemption.

## Conclusion: a viable but regulation-intensive path forward

BodyCypher's dual-entity model is the right structural choice. The tech entity can achieve **100% foreign ownership through BOI Activity 8.1.1** with accessible capital requirements, while the medical entity provides the healthcare licensing necessary for doctor-supervised services. The founder's 3–5 million THB budget is sufficient for BOI promotion, given the salary-based investment metric for digital businesses.

Three risks dominate the regulatory landscape. First, the **classification boundary** between wellness technology and medical practice is the single most consequential determination — it triggers cascading regulatory consequences across the Medical Facility Act, Medical Profession Act, FDA regulations, and liability frameworks. The founder should seek formal FDA classification of the software and structure the app's outputs to stay firmly on the wellness/educational side. Second, **liability for adverse health outcomes cannot be disclaimed** under Thai law, and the burden of proof falls on the company — making comprehensive insurance and careful Terms of Service structuring essential rather than optional. Third, **PDPA compliance for health data** requires immediate DPO appointment, granular consent mechanisms, and careful management of cross-border data flows to AI APIs.

The regulatory environment is evolving rapidly. Thailand's Draft AI Bill, ongoing FBA liberalization proposals, and the PDPC's aggressive enforcement trajectory all signal a tightening regime. The founder should engage experienced Thai legal counsel specializing in health tech, submit for FDA software classification early, and build compliance infrastructure before launch rather than retrofitting it.
