# Corporate structuring playbook for a SEA healthtech expansion

**[[bodycypher|BodyCypher]]'s optimal path starts with a dual-entity structure in Thailand — a BOI-promoted tech company paired with a Thai-majority medical entity — anchored under a Singapore holding company that owns all IP and technology.** This structure solves three problems at once: it satisfies Thai foreign ownership restrictions, creates a clean IP licensing channel for profit repatriation, and positions the company for regional [[fundraise|fundraising]] from day one. Doctor Anywhere, the closest comparable company, used exactly this Singapore-holdco-plus-local-OpCos model to expand across six SEA countries, raising over **US$180 million** through its Singapore entity. The regulatory landscape is shifting fast — Thailand proposed FBA reform in April 2025, Indonesia opened 100% foreign hospital ownership in February 2025, and Vietnam formalized telemedicine law in January 2024 — making 2026 a uniquely favorable window for a structured regional entry.

---

## The Singapore holding company is non-negotiable

The standard corporate architecture used by virtually every regional SEA healthtech company follows a clear hierarchy: a Singapore Pte Ltd sits at the top, owning all intellectual property, brand, and technology. Below it, wholly or partially owned operating companies in each country handle local operations, medical licensing, and customer relationships. Doctor Anywhere, Grab, Hello Health Group, WhiteCoat Global, and Docquity all use this model.

**Why Singapore specifically?** The advantages are concrete and quantifiable. Singapore charges a **17% headline corporate tax rate** with no capital gains tax and no dividend withholding tax — meaning profits flow cleanly to shareholders. The country maintains **90+ double taxation agreements** covering every SEA market. For IP income specifically, the IP Development Incentive can reduce the effective tax rate on software royalties to as low as **5%** if R&D is conducted in Singapore. The Enterprise Innovation Scheme provides **400% tax deductions** on qualifying R&D expenditure and IP registration costs through 2028.

For investors, Singapore is the default jurisdiction. Singapore-based companies captured roughly **64% of total SEA deal value** in recent years. VCs structured for SEA — Sequoia Capital Southeast Asia, Vertex Ventures, East Ventures, Asia Partners — all invest through Singapore Pte Ltd entities. The common law legal system, familiar share structures (preference shares, convertible notes, SAFEs), and zero capital gains tax on exit make it the path of least resistance for fundraising.

Setting up a lean Singapore holdco costs approximately **S$3,000–S$8,000 in the first year** and **S$5,000–S$12,000 annually** thereafter (company secretary, registered address, tax filing, bookkeeping). A nominee local director runs S$2,000–S$5,000 per year if the founder isn't Singapore-resident. The small company audit exemption applies to most early-stage holdcos (revenue under S$10 million). One critical compliance point: from 2025, IRAS tightened Certificate of Residence requirements. The holdco must demonstrate genuine **control and management exercised in Singapore** — board meetings held there, strategic decisions made by a Singapore-based person — not just a paper entity with nominees.

## Thailand's dual-entity structure protects both the founder and the business

Thailand presents a regulatory puzzle with three interlocking pieces: the Foreign Business Act, the Medical Establishment Act, and BOI promotion. The solution is a **dual-entity structure** that separates technology from medical operations.

**Entity 1: BOI-promoted technology company (up to 100% foreign-owned).** This entity develops and operates the AI platform, manages subscriptions, handles customer acquisition, and licenses technology from the Singapore holdco. Under **BOI Category 8.1.1** (Development of Software, Digital Platform, or Digital Content), it receives up to **8 years of corporate income tax exemption**, exemption from the FBA's 49% foreign ownership cap, streamlined work permits without the standard 4:1 Thai-to-foreigner employee ratio, and elimination of the THB 2 million capital-per-work-permit requirement. The key condition: software development must be done in Thailand, with minimum **THB 1.5 million annual salary** expenditure on Thai IT personnel. BOI application typically takes **2–4 months**.

**Entity 2: Thai-majority medical company (49% foreign / 51% Thai doctor co-founder).** This entity holds the medical establishment license, operates or partners with the licensed clinic, supervises blood testing, and employs licensed medical professionals. The Medical Establishment Act requires that any entity operating a clinic or lab have its operator license held by a **Thai-licensed medical professional**. This entity handles all regulated medical activities — blood draws, lab work, medical interpretation of results, and any telemedicine consultations. The Thai doctor co-founder serves as both licensee and medical director.

These two entities connect through **service agreements**: the tech company provides the platform, customer management, and AI analytics to the medical entity; the medical entity provides clinical services to the tech company's customers. Revenue flows primarily through the tech entity (subscriptions), with the medical entity compensated through service fees for clinical work.

**The FBA's teeth are real.** In September 2024, **23 defendants received 5-year prison sentences** and THB 200,000 fines for nominee arrangements. Over **26,000 businesses** have been flagged as suspected nominees, with 861 cases under active investigation. Proposed amendments would classify serious FBA violations as **predicate offenses for money laundering**, enabling AMLO asset seizure. The Thai government is deploying AI tools to cross-reference company registrations with tax records in real time. The practical implication: BodyCypher's Thai doctor co-founder must be a genuine partner with real capital investment, real economic participation, and real involvement in the business. Preference share structures giving foreigners disproportionate control are under increasing scrutiny.

**Supplement delivery requires its own regulatory track.** Health supplements fall under the **Food Act B.E. 2522** and must be registered with the Thai FDA before sale. Import licenses require a Thai-registered entity with an FDA-approved warehouse. Registration takes **2–4 months** for standard products. Retail supplement sales are specifically **excluded from BOI digital services promotion**, meaning this component must flow through the Thai-majority entity or a licensed distributor partner. Advertising health claims is strictly regulated under the Consumer Protection Act.

**AI health recommendations walk a regulatory tightrope.** Thailand's FDA has issued guidelines distinguishing medical device software (requiring registration) from general wellness software (not regulated). The safest positioning for BodyCypher: frame AI outputs as **"health optimization insights" and "wellness suggestions"** rather than medical diagnoses. Have all AI-generated recommendations reviewed and endorsed by the Thai doctor co-founder before delivery. The licensed clinic issues formal medical interpretation of blood results. Thailand is drafting a comprehensive Digital Health Act, but it remains in development as of early 2026.

## Doctor Anywhere proves the model works — and reveals its limits

Doctor Anywhere, founded in 2016 by former Temasek investor Lim Wai Mun with S$150,000 of personal savings, is the closest precedent to what BodyCypher aims to build. The company now operates across **six SEA countries** with **2.5+ million users**, **3,000+ doctors**, and **SGD 81.7 million in 2023 revenue**.

The Thailand expansion is the most instructive case study. In November 2021, Doctor Anywhere **acquired Doctor Raksa**, then Thailand's largest telemedicine platform with over 1 million registered users and 1,000+ doctors. Doctor Raksa was itself structured with a **Singaporean parent company** — even as a Thai-focused business, its founders chose Singapore as the holding jurisdiction. The acquisition gave DA instant market leadership, existing regulatory compliance, hospital partnerships (notably with Bumrungrad Hospital), and a locally trusted brand. Post-acquisition, DA **retained the Doctor Raksa brand** alongside the Doctor Anywhere name, hired a Thai general manager (Surangkhana Surapaitoon), and kept the existing local team intact. The combined entity targeted **5x revenue growth** in Thailand within 24 months.

Critically, DA uses **different entry strategies for different markets**. In Thailand, it acquired. In the Philippines, it formed a **joint venture with Equicom Group**, a local conglomerate with HMO and fintech operations. In Malaysia and Vietnam, it expanded organically with local offices. In India, it established a tech hub only (no patient-facing operations). This market-by-market adaptation reflects a key reality: healthcare regulations are so locally specific that no single expansion template works everywhere.

Three other case studies reinforce different aspects of the model. **WhiteCoat Global** (Singapore) acquired Good Doctor Indonesia in October 2024 — the largest telehealth M&A in SEA history — demonstrating that acquisition-led expansion can rapidly build regional scale. **MyDoc** (Singapore) partnered with Prudential to expand via the Pulse by Prudential app across **11 markets**, showing that B2B distribution through insurers enables light-touch multi-market entry. **Halodoc** (Indonesia) has raised **US$258 million** but remains focused almost entirely on Indonesia, illustrating that even well-funded companies find cross-border expansion daunting enough to delay it.

The sobering data point: Doctor Anywhere, despite US$180M+ in funding and SGD 81.7M revenue, **still runs at a net loss of SGD 23.5 million** (2023). DA's key strategic pivot was recognizing that "physical presence is very important in order to build trust with users" — moving from pure telehealth to an omnichannel model with clinics, health screening centers, and specialist care. For BodyCypher, this suggests the blood testing and clinic partnership components aren't just regulatory necessities — they're strategic advantages that build the trust digital-only platforms struggle to establish.

## The Thailand JV with a doctor co-founder needs ironclad protections

BodyCypher's model — tech platform partnered with a licensed clinic for medical components — is well-precedented in Thailand. **BeWell Asia** runs a direct-to-consumer blood testing service in Bangkok and Chiang Mai by partnering with Thai laboratories; customers order online, visit a partner lab for blood draws, and receive physician consultations for result interpretation. **Health Deliver Asia** offers home-visit blood draws through licensed nurses plus clinic visits, partnering with internationally certified laboratories. **Blez Clinic** partners with N Health (a subsidiary of [[bdms-wellness|BDMS]], Thailand's largest hospital network) for laboratory testing.

The recommended structure is a **service/management agreement model** rather than a traditional equity JV for the medical component. The tech company provides the platform, handles customer acquisition, processes payments, manages subscriptions, and handles supplement logistics. The licensed clinic handles blood collection and testing supervision, medical professional oversight, telemedicine consultations, regulatory compliance for medical services, and formal interpretation of results. Revenue flows primarily through the tech entity, with the clinic compensated through service fees — typically **15–30% of revenue** for technology/management fees, or a per-transaction fee for each blood test and consultation processed.

The contractual architecture must include five critical protections for the tech company:

- **IP ownership clauses** ensuring all technology, algorithms, customer data, and brand assets remain with the foreign tech company (or Singapore holdco), never the Thai medical entity
- **Put and call options** with pre-agreed triggers (breach, deadlock, performance failure) allowing either party to buy or sell shares at a pre-agreed valuation methodology
- **Non-compete and exclusivity provisions** preventing the doctor/clinic from partnering with competing platforms, with reasonable geographic and time limitations (2–3 years within Thailand)
- **Backup physician provisions** ensuring the business doesn't collapse if the primary doctor relationship breaks down — the clinic license should be in the company's name where possible, with multiple licensed physicians available
- **Singapore-law arbitration clauses** — Thailand is a New York Convention signatory, making SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre) awards enforceable

The most critical structural risk: **if the Thai doctor co-founder departs, the medical establishment license could be revoked**. Mitigations include having the clinic license held in the company name rather than personally, building relationships with additional licensed physicians, and establishing vesting schedules on any equity granted to the doctor. The shareholder agreement should specify reserved matters requiring both parties' consent for major decisions (budget changes, new hires, IP disposition, new service lines) even though the Thai partner holds 51%.

## Country-by-country expansion difficulty varies dramatically

Not all SEA markets are created equal for a foreign healthtech company. Based on foreign ownership rules, regulatory clarity, market size, and practical barriers, the six target countries rank from easiest to hardest:

**Singapore (easiest)** allows 100% foreign ownership with no restrictions. The Healthcare Services Act 2020 provides clear licensing for telemedicine and laboratory services. Supplements require no pre-market approval. Company registration takes 1–2 days. The drawback: a small domestic market of **5.9 million** and high operating costs. Best used as regional HQ and premium market rather than primary growth engine.

**Malaysia** permits 100% foreign ownership in private healthcare since a 2012 liberalization. It has a dedicated **Telemedicine Act since 1997** — one of only two SEA countries with specific telemedicine legislation. English is widely spoken. MIDA actively courts healthtech investment. The **33 million population** and strong medical tourism sector make it an attractive second market after Thailand.

**Thailand** is the most complex regulatory environment for BodyCypher but also the home market. The 49% foreign ownership cap is the main barrier, but BOI promotion unlocks 100% ownership. The proposed **FBA reform** (approved by Cabinet in April 2025) could significantly ease restrictions by 2026, driven by Thailand's OECD membership bid. A **70 million population** and established medical tourism infrastructure make the fundamentals strong.

**Vietnam** allows 100% foreign ownership for healthtech and formalized telemedicine law in January 2024. The **100 million population** and 10% annual healthcare spending growth are compelling. However, pharmaceutical and supplement distribution is **completely prohibited for foreign-invested enterprises** — a critical barrier for BodyCypher's supplement delivery component that would require a local distribution partner. Infrastructure gaps outside major cities and an evolving regulatory framework add complexity.

**Philippines** permits 100% foreign ownership for healthtech (not on the negative list). English is widely spoken, and the **115 million population** is attractive. The Constitutional restriction that medical practice is limited to Filipino citizens creates barriers, and the **US$200,000 minimum capital** requirement for domestic market enterprises is relatively high. FDA registration backlogs slow supplement approvals.

**Indonesia (hardest but biggest opportunity)** recently opened 100% foreign hospital ownership and reduced minimum paid-up capital to **IDR 2.5 billion (~US$150,000)**. But the total investment requirement of **IDR 10 billion (~US$600,000)** per project, multi-layered bureaucracy, geographic fragmentation across 17,000+ islands, and rigorous BPOM supplement registration make it the most complex market. The payoff: **275 million people** and a connected healthcare market growing at **27.4% CAGR** to an estimated US$3.81 billion by 2031.

## How to move profits efficiently through IP licensing

The IP licensing structure between the Singapore holdco and local operating entities serves as the primary profit repatriation channel. Here's how the mechanics work for BodyCypher:

The Thai operating entity earns revenue from subscriptions. It pays a **5–8% royalty** on net revenue to the Singapore holdco for the right to use BodyCypher's AI platform, algorithms, brand, and proprietary technology. This royalty is a **tax-deductible expense** in Thailand, reducing Thai taxable income (which would otherwise face 20% CIT). The royalty payment triggers withholding tax, but the Singapore-Thailand DTA reduces this to **5% for software copyright** (versus 15% domestic rate) or **8% for patents and technology**. In Singapore, the royalty income is taxed at 17% CIT, but the Thai withholding tax paid is available as a **foreign tax credit**, preventing double taxation.

The net effect: profits shift from a 20% tax jurisdiction (Thailand) to a 17% jurisdiction (Singapore) with no capital gains tax and no dividend withholding tax. If the Singapore holdco qualifies for the IP Development Incentive, the effective tax on IP income drops to as low as **5–10%**. The royalty rate must be defensible under transfer pricing rules — IRAS follows OECD guidelines and will benchmark against comparable arm's length transactions using industry databases. A combined technology-plus-brand license at **5–8% of net revenue** falls within typical defensible ranges for healthtech platforms.

Singapore's DTA network provides similar benefits across all target markets. Withholding tax on royalties drops from domestic rates to treaty rates: **8% for Malaysia** (versus 10% domestic), **10% for Indonesia** (versus 20% domestic), **5–10% for Vietnam** (versus 10% domestic), and **15% for the Philippines** (versus 25–30% domestic). The Philippines offers the least DTA benefit among SEA countries.

Transfer pricing documentation is mandatory for Singapore companies with gross revenue exceeding S$10 million. Below that threshold, documentation is technically exempt but the arm's length principle still applies. The practical advice: **maintain transfer pricing documentation from day one**, even at early stage. It costs relatively little to prepare and protects against future challenges from both IRAS and local tax authorities.

## Start with BodyCypher, consider local brands for expansion

The brand strategy decision has structural implications. Doctor Anywhere uses a single brand across all markets (though it retained the Doctor Raksa name in Thailand post-acquisition). Halodoc has stayed single-market, single-brand. The hybrid "powered by" approach — where each country has a locally resonant brand powered by BodyCypher's technology — offers the best balance for BodyCypher's specific situation.

**For Thailand (the home market)**, use the BodyCypher brand directly. The initial target audience is English-speaking expatriates and cosmopolitan Thais in Bangkok — a demographic that responds well to a global-feeling health optimization brand. The brand builds equity from the start and establishes BodyCypher as a recognized name.

**For subsequent markets**, consider the "powered by" model: a locally named brand (possibly co-branded with the local doctor/clinic partner) that runs on BodyCypher's technology platform. This approach provides a **regulatory firewall** between countries (if Thailand faces issues, the Vietnam brand is unaffected), allows **individual country exits** (you can sell the Malaysia operation independently), enables **local market customization** (different languages, cultural positioning, pricing), and still builds value in the parent company's IP and technology.

Asia Partners, a major SEA growth equity investor, noted a crucial point: "A successful outcome in an export market in a four to five-year investment period cannot belong to the base case. It always has to be an upside case." At seed and Series A, investors will evaluate BodyCypher primarily on its **Thailand performance**. The regional expansion capability should be architected into the corporate structure and technology platform from day one, but the brand story for early fundraising should center on Thailand execution with regional expansion as upside.

## What to do on day one

The recommended corporate structure and sequencing for BodyCypher:

**Immediate (Month 1–3):** Incorporate **BodyCypher Pte. Ltd.** in Singapore as the holding company. Register all IP, trademarks, and technology ownership here. Cost: approximately S$3,000–S$5,000. Simultaneously, incorporate the Thai medical entity as a Thai Co., Ltd. with the doctor co-founder holding 51% and the foreign founder (or the Singapore holdco) holding 49%. Begin the **BOI application** under Category 8.1.1 for the technology/AI platform component.

**Short-term (Month 3–6):** Execute the IP license agreement between Singapore holdco and the Thai medical entity (or a separate Thai tech entity once BOI-promoted). Set royalty rate at 5–7% of net revenue, documented with transfer pricing analysis. Execute service agreements between the tech entity and medical entity. Ensure the Thai doctor co-founder obtains the medical establishment license for the clinic. Partner with an existing licensed laboratory for blood testing (outsourcing initially, rather than building a lab). Begin Thai FDA supplement registration for the product line.

**Medium-term (Month 6–12):** Once BOI promotion is received, consider establishing the separate BOI-promoted tech entity for the AI platform, with the Singapore holdco as majority or sole shareholder. Apply for work permits through the BOI one-stop service center. Explore the **Smart-S Visa** through NIA or DEPA certification if the founder's current visa arrangement is suboptimal.

**Longer-term (Year 2+):** When considering expansion, evaluate Malaysia first (100% ownership, English-speaking, Telemedicine Act, proximity), then Vietnam (100% ownership for tech, large market, but needs local supplement distribution partner). For each new market, replicate the structure: local operating entity, local doctor/clinic partner, IP license from Singapore holdco, and service agreements between tech and medical components.

The critical principle throughout: **build the regional architecture from day one** (Singapore holdco, IP centralization, standardized licensing agreements) even though execution starts in a single market. Retrofitting a Singapore holding structure onto an existing Thai company is more expensive, more complex, and potentially triggers tax events. The incremental cost of getting the structure right at the start — roughly **S$5,000–S$10,000 above** what a Thailand-only structure would cost — is trivial compared to the restructuring costs and investor friction that a suboptimal structure creates later.

## Conclusion

The regulatory landscape across SEA is moving toward greater openness for foreign healthtech investment — Thailand's proposed FBA reform, Indonesia's 100% ownership for hospitals, Vietnam's formalized telemedicine law — but each country retains enough complexity to require market-specific structuring. BodyCypher's most defensible approach treats Thailand as both home market and proof of concept, uses the Singapore holdco as the IP and fundraising vehicle from inception, and builds a repeatable playbook (local doctor partner + BOI or equivalent promotion + service agreement model) that can be adapted per market. The dual-entity structure in Thailand — BOI tech company plus Thai-majority medical entity — isn't just a regulatory workaround; it reflects the genuine division between the technology product (which is borderless and scalable) and the medical service delivery (which is inherently local and regulated). Doctor Anywhere's trajectory, from four employees to six countries in eight years, shows this model can scale — but its ongoing losses at SGD 81.7 million revenue also demonstrate that regional healthtech requires patient capital and a willingness to prioritize market-by-market execution over rapid geographic spread.
