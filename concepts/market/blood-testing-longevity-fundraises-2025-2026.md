# $1.2B+ poured into blood testing and longevity health in 18 months

**The direct-to-consumer blood testing, biomarker, and longevity diagnostics market has exploded with funding activity since early 2025.** At least **$1.2 billion in confirmed venture funding** has flowed to companies in this space across 25+ deals, producing two new unicorns (Function Health at $2.5B and Neko Health at $1.8B). When adjacent longevity therapeutics and strategic acquisitions are included, the total capital deployed exceeds **$2 billion**. Every major VC firm — a16z, Lightspeed, Forerunner, Accel, Khosla, General Catalyst — has placed bets. Celebrity investors from Kim Kardashian to Cristiano Ronaldo to Matt Damon are piling in. And massive consumer brands like Hims & Hers, Oura, and Whoop are racing to launch their own lab testing products. This is not a niche trend — it is a full-blown market breakout.

---

## The mega-rounds: two unicorns minted in 12 months

The headline numbers tell the story. **Function Health** closed a **$298M Series B** in November 2025 at a **$2.5B valuation**, led by Redpoint Ventures with participation from a16z, Battery Ventures, Menlo Ventures, Samsung Next, and celebrity investors including Matt Damon, Magic Johnson, and Zac Efron. The company offers 160+ biomarker lab tests for $365/year at 2,000+ Quest Diagnostics locations and acquired full-body MRI startup **Ezra** in May 2025 to combine blood testing with imaging. Function has processed **50M+ lab tests** since its 2023 launch and reportedly runs at ~$100M in annual revenue.

Just ten months earlier, **Neko Health** — co-founded by Spotify's Daniel Ek — raised a **$260M Series B** at a **$1.8B valuation** in January 2025, led by Lightspeed with General Catalyst and Atomico participating. Neko's AI-powered body scan collects **50 million data points per session** across thermal cameras, 3D mapping, ECG, and lasers. With 100,000+ on its waitlist and an 80% retention rate, Neko is expanding from Stockholm and London to New York in 2026.

**Prenuvo** quietly raised **$120M** (announced February 2025) from Forerunner, Left Lane Capital, and Felicis for its whole-body MRI screening business. The company crossed **$100M in revenue** in 2024, is profitable, and has screened 110,000+ members. It is now adding blood biomarker testing to its scan offerings.

These three rounds alone total **$678M** and signal that investors view preventive health screening as a massive consumer category, not a medical niche.

## The smart money is all-in: who's investing and why it matters

The investor roster reads like a who's who of venture capital. **Forerunner Ventures** led or co-led rounds for Superpower, Prenuvo, and Tally Health — a clear thesis bet on consumer preventive health. **Andreessen Horowitz** backed both Function Health and Truemed. **Lightspeed** led Neko's $260M round. **Accel** made its *first-ever longevity investment* by leading Generation Lab's seed. **Khosla Ventures** led Circulate Health's seed. **Left Lane Capital** co-led Prenuvo and led Everlab's seed.

Celebrity and operator capital is flooding in at unprecedented scale. Bryan Johnson's Blueprint round attracted **Kim Kardashian, Paris Hilton, Naval Ravikant, Balaji Srinivasan, Alex Hormozi**, and the Winklevoss twins. Function Health drew **Matt Damon, Magic Johnson, Joel Embiid, and Roku CEO Anthony Wood**. Superpower counted **Giannis Antetokounmpo, Logan Paul, and Vanessa Hudgens** among its backers. Bioniq secured investments from **Cristiano Ronaldo and Manchester United's Diogo Dalot**.

This breadth of investor types — top-tier VCs, strategic corporates (Samsung Next, Dexcom), and mainstream celebrities — signals that blood testing has crossed from health-niche into mainstream consumer category.

## Corporate giants are racing to catch up

Perhaps the strongest signal of market momentum is that **incumbent consumer health companies are acquiring and launching aggressively** to avoid being left behind.

**Hims & Hers** (NYSE: HIMS, ~$7B market cap) made two acquisitions in 2025 to enter blood testing: **Trybe Labs** in February 2025 (at-home blood draw capability) and **YourBio Health** in late 2025 (painless microneedle blood collection). The company then launched its own "Labs" product with plans for **50–120+ biomarker panels** via Quest Diagnostics. CEO Andrew Dudum publicly stated he expects labs to become a **$1 billion business line**.

**Oura** (valued at $5.2B after its 2024 Series D) launched Health Panels in October 2025 — **$99 blood tests** at Quest locations, integrated with ring data and its AI Advisor. **Whoop** opened its Advanced Labs blood testing service to its 350,000-person waitlist in September 2025. Both moves validate the thesis that wearable data alone is insufficient — consumers want blood biomarkers too.

**Function Health acquired Ezra** (full-body MRI, $44M previously raised) in May 2025, creating a combined blood testing + imaging platform. **Superpower acquired Base** (biomarker testing) in June 2025 and **Feminade** (women's health) in January 2025. Consolidation is accelerating.

## Adjacent longevity funding amplifies the signal

The blood testing wave sits within an even larger longevity funding surge. **NewLimit** (Brian Armstrong, Coinbase co-founder) raised **$130M Series B** in May 2025 for epigenetic reprogramming. **Retro Biosciences** (Sam Altman-backed) is reportedly raising **~$1B at a $5B valuation**. **Insilico Medicine** closed a **$123M Series E** for AI-driven drug discovery targeting aging. These longevity therapeutics companies validate the same consumer demand that drives blood testing adoption: people want to understand and optimize their biological health.

The broader market data confirms the trend. US digital health funding hit **$14.2 billion in 2025** — the highest since 2022 — with mega-deals over $100M accounting for **42% of all funding**, the highest proportion since 2021. The DTC lab testing market specifically is valued at **$3.5B in 2025** and projected to reach **$6–8B by 2030**.

## Conclusion: the window is open now

Three patterns make the urgency unmistakable. First, the **speed of scaling**: Function Health went from launch to $2.5B valuation and ~$100M revenue run rate in roughly two years. Superpower accumulated 150,000 waitlist signups before even launching. Second, the **convergence of modalities**: the winning companies are combining blood biomarkers with MRI, wearables, genetics, and AI interpretation into unified platforms — Function acquired Ezra, Prenuvo added blood testing, Oura and Whoop launched labs. Third, the **price war** signals mass-market intent: Superpower dropped from $499 to $199/year, Function cut to $365, and Oura launched at $99 per panel.

The companies funded in 2025 are not building for a niche wellness audience. They are building the consumer health infrastructure of the next decade — and the capital markets have made a decisive bet that the category is real, massive, and moving fast. The question is no longer whether this market will materialize. It is whether new entrants can move quickly enough to claim a position before incumbents and well-funded startups lock up distribution, data, and consumer trust.
